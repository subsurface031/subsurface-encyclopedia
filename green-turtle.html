Chelonia mydas — Green Turtle, KZN

Green Turtle

Chelonia mydas ⚠ Endangered

The green turtle is the largest of the hard-shelled sea turtles and one of the most ecologically important marine animals in KwaZulu-Natal's coastal waters. Despite its name, the green turtle's shell is not green — it is typically brown, olive, or black with attractive streaked patterning. The "green" refers instead to the distinctive greenish tint of the fat and cartilage beneath the skin, a result of its herbivorous diet of seagrass and algae.

Green turtles are regular foragers along the KZN coastline, grazing the seagrass beds and algae-covered reefs from iSimangaliso southward to the Durban bight. They are a frequent and beloved sight for divers and snorkellers throughout KZN's northern reefs — calm, unhurried, and often tolerant of careful human observers. While they nest on the iSimangaliso beaches in smaller numbers than loggerheads, their ecological role as marine grazers makes them irreplaceable components of healthy coastal ecosystems.

Shell Length Up to 1.5 m
Max Weight ~230 kg
Lifespan 80+ years
Nesting Season Nov – Feb
Eggs per Nest ~115 eggs
Maturity Age 25 – 35 years

Physical Description

The green turtle has a smooth, streamlined carapace that is broader and more rounded than the loggerhead's. The shell is typically brown to olive in colour with a beautiful radiating pattern of lighter streaks and blotches — each individual's pattern is unique, much like a fingerprint. The plastron is pale yellow to white. Adults have a small, rounded head with a single pair of prefrontal scales between the eyes — a key distinguishing feature from other sea turtle species.

Their jaws are finely serrated — an adaptation for cropping seagrass and scraping algae from hard surfaces. Unlike the loggerhead's massive crushing jaws, the green turtle's beak is relatively narrow and more delicate, reflecting its exclusively plant-based adult diet.

Males are distinguished from females by a longer, more prominent tail that extends well beyond the rear of the shell, and by the presence of a single curved claw on each front flipper used during mating. Females are generally slightly larger than males in overall body size.

Behaviour

Green turtles are the only sea turtle species that undergoes a dramatic dietary shift during its life cycle. Juveniles in their early oceanic phase are carnivorous, feeding on jellyfish, crustaceans, and other invertebrates at the surface of the open ocean. As they mature and transition to nearshore habitats, they progressively shift to an almost exclusively herbivorous diet — a transition that is unique among sea turtles and parallels a complete change in habitat, physiology, and behaviour.

Adult green turtles are largely solitary foragers but aggregate in large numbers at productive feeding grounds and at nesting beaches. They are capable of remaining submerged for several hours when resting, lowering their heart rate dramatically to conserve oxygen. They have been observed sleeping on the seafloor or wedged under coral ledges for extended periods.

Green turtles are the most commonly encountered sea turtle species by divers in KZN's northern reef systems. They are generally placid and approachable, though disturbance during feeding or rest should always be avoided. Feeding green turtles at dive sites — a practice that still occurs at some locations — is discouraged as it disrupts natural behaviour and creates dependency.

Diet & Feeding

Adult green turtles are the only exclusively herbivorous sea turtles, and their role as marine grazers gives them an ecological function analogous to terrestrial grazing mammals. By cropping seagrass blades short and regularly, green turtles prevent seagrass beds from becoming overgrown and senescent — stimulating new growth, improving nutrient cycling, and maintaining the productivity of one of the ocean's most important nursery habitats.

In KZN, green turtles graze the seagrass meadows of the iSimangaliso estuary system and the shallow banks along the northern coast, as well as cropping algae from reef surfaces throughout the Sodwana Bay area. A healthy green turtle population is considered an essential indicator of seagrass ecosystem health.

  • Seagrasses (primary adult diet)
  • Macro-algae and reef algae
  • Jellyfish and invertebrates (juveniles)
  • Sponges and tunicates (occasionally)
  • Carrion (very rarely)

Nesting & Reproduction

Green turtles nesting in KZN use the same iSimangaliso beaches as loggerheads and leatherbacks, with the nesting season running from November through February. They nest in smaller numbers than loggerheads in KZN — typically a few dozen to a few hundred females per season — but are important contributors to the beach's overall turtle nesting activity. Each female deposits approximately 115 eggs per clutch and may nest two to five times per season.

Green turtles are among the most faithful of all sea turtles to their natal beaches — some females have been recorded nesting on the same KZN beach for over 30 years. They also undertake some of the longest migrations of any sea turtle to reach their nesting grounds, with individuals feeding as far away as the East African coast, Mozambique Channel, and even further afield returning to iSimangaliso to nest.

Sexual maturity is not reached until 25 to 35 years of age — the latest of any of the three KZN nesting species — meaning that the green turtles nesting on iSimangaliso beaches today hatched in the 1990s or earlier, a sobering reminder of how long it takes for conservation efforts to show results in this species.

Habitat & Distribution in KZN

Green turtles are found throughout KwaZulu-Natal's coastal waters, from the Mozambique border southward. They are most abundant along the northern KZN coast where productive seagrass beds and coral reefs provide year-round foraging habitat, particularly around the iSimangaliso Wetland Park, Sodwana Bay, and the Two-Mile and Five-Mile reef systems. They are regularly encountered by divers at Aliwal Shoal further south, and occasional individuals are sighted as far south as the Durban and south coast waters, particularly during warmer summer months.

The iSimangaliso estuarine system — including Lake St. Lucia — provides critical seagrass foraging habitat that supports resident green turtle populations year-round. The turtles that feed here are not necessarily the same individuals that nest on nearby beaches; some may have travelled thousands of kilometres from nesting beaches elsewhere in the Indian Ocean to feed in these productive waters.

Green turtles face threats from bycatch in trawl and gillnet fisheries, entanglement in beach seine nets, ingestion of plastic debris, fibropapillomatosis (a debilitating viral disease causing tumour growth), boat strike, and loss of seagrass foraging habitat through coastal pollution and water quality degradation. Historical hunting for their meat and eggs — the original source of the name "green turtle" came from the prized green-coloured fat used to make turtle soup — caused catastrophic population declines from which many populations are still slowly recovering.

Fascinating Facts

Named for Green Fat

Despite what the name suggests, the green turtle's shell is brown or olive — not green. The name comes from the distinctive greenish colour of the fat layer beneath the skin, caused by their seagrass and algae diet. It was this fat that made green turtles a prized food source for sailors and contributed to their near-extinction in many parts of the world.

Only Herbivorous Sea Turtle

Adult green turtles are the only sea turtle species that feeds exclusively on plants. This dietary specialisation makes them the ocean's equivalent of grazing mammals — their seagrass grazing activity is essential for maintaining healthy, productive seagrass meadows that serve as nursery habitat for hundreds of fish species.

25 – 35 Year Wait

Green turtles take longer to reach sexual maturity than any other KZN sea turtle — up to 35 years in some populations. A green turtle nesting on iSimangaliso's beaches today may have hatched before the year 2000. This extraordinary delay means that the benefits of current conservation efforts won't be measurable for decades.

Sleeping Underwater

Green turtles can slow their heart rate to as little as one beat per minute when resting underwater, allowing them to remain submerged for several hours without surfacing to breathe. They are frequently found sleeping wedged under coral ledges on KZN's reefs — a behaviour that leaves them vulnerable to entanglement in fishing gear.

Fibropapillomatosis

Green turtles are particularly susceptible to fibropapillomatosis — a viral disease that causes large, disfiguring tumours on the skin, flippers, and eyes. The disease has reached epidemic proportions in some populations and is associated with degraded coastal water quality. It is one of the most significant health threats facing green turtles in KZN's inshore waters.

Seagrass Farmers

By regularly cropping seagrass blades to a short, productive length, green turtles effectively "farm" their feeding grounds — stimulating faster regrowth and higher nutritional content in the grass. Seagrass beds in areas with healthy green turtle populations are demonstrably more productive than ungrazed beds, highlighting the turtle's role as an ecosystem engineer.
