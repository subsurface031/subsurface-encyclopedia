Humpback Whale

Megaptera novaeangliae ✓ Least Concern

The humpback whale is one of the most iconic and beloved marine mammals on Earth — celebrated for its haunting songs, spectacular acrobatic displays, and remarkable annual migrations that span entire ocean basins. In KwaZulu-Natal, humpbacks are the stars of the whale-watching season, migrating past the coastline every winter en route between their Antarctic feeding grounds and the warm tropical waters off Mozambique and Madagascar where they breed and calve.

From May through December, these gentle giants pass within sight of KZN's shores, often breaching, tail-slapping, and displaying just metres from the beach. Locations like St. Lucia, Sodwana Bay, and Durban's Bluff offer some of the world's most reliable and accessible humpback whale encounters — a phenomenon that has established Durban as one of the globe's first certified Whale Heritage Sites in recognition of its commitment to cetacean conservation and responsible whale tourism.

Max Length 16 m
Max Weight ~40 tonnes
Lifespan 45 – 50 years
Migration Season May – Dec
Migration Distance ~5,000 km
Gestation Period ~12 months

Physical Description

Humpback whales are immediately recognizable by their stocky bodies, distinctive humped backs from which they derive their name, and extraordinarily long pectoral fins — the longest appendages in the animal kingdom relative to body size, often spanning up to one-third of the whale's total body length. These fins can exceed 5 metres in large adults and are stark white on the underside, creating dramatic flashes when the whale rolls or breaches.

The body is predominantly black or dark grey on the dorsal surface, fading to lighter mottling on the belly and throat. The head and lower jaw are covered in distinctive knobby protuberances called tubercles, each containing a single sensory hair follicle. The trailing edge of the tail flukes is uniquely scalloped and deeply notched, with distinctive black-and-white pigmentation patterns on the underside that are as unique as human fingerprints — allowing researchers to identify individual whales.

Adult females are slightly larger than males. Humpbacks migrating past KZN typically measure 12 to 16 metres in length and weigh between 27 and 40 tonnes, though exceptional individuals can exceed these averages. Their massive hearts weigh approximately 195 kg — as much as two adult humans.

Behaviour

Humpbacks are the most acrobatic of all large whales, famous for spectacular surface behaviours including breaching — launching their entire 30 to 40-tonne bodies clear of the water — sometimes repeatedly for hours. They also engage in lobtailing (slapping the water with their tail flukes), pec-slapping (striking the surface with their long pectoral fins), and spyhopping (lifting their heads vertically out of the water to observe their surroundings).

The purpose of these displays remains debated — they may serve as communication, parasite removal, play, or simply exuberance — but they create unforgettable encounters for whale watchers along the KZN coast. Humpbacks are also among the most vocal of all cetaceans. Males produce complex, haunting songs lasting 10 to 20 minutes that can be repeated for hours. These songs — unique to each population and evolving slowly over time — can be heard underwater up to 30 kilometres away.

Humpbacks are generally curious and inquisitive around boats, occasionally approaching vessels to investigate. While usually solitary or in small groups during migration, they sometimes aggregate in feeding or socializing groups of 10 or more individuals.

Diet & Feeding

Humpback whales are baleen whales — they lack teeth and instead possess 270 to 400 baleen plates hanging from their upper jaws that act as enormous sieves to filter prey from seawater. They are seasonal feeders, gorging themselves on vast quantities of krill, small fish, and other zooplankton during the austral summer in the nutrient-rich polar waters near Antarctica, then fasting almost entirely during the winter breeding season in tropical waters.

A feeding humpback can consume up to 2,500 kg of krill per day. They employ sophisticated cooperative feeding strategies including bubble-net feeding — a technique where groups of whales swim in coordinated circles below a school of prey while exhaling streams of bubbles that form cylindrical "nets" to concentrate the fish. The whales then lunge upward through the center with mouths agape, engulfing tonnes of water and prey in a single gulp.

  • Antarctic krill (primary diet in feeding grounds)
  • Small schooling fish (mackerel, herring, capelin, sand eel)
  • Copepods and other zooplankton
  • Opportunistic feeding during migration (minimal)

Migration & Reproduction

Humpbacks undertake one of the longest migrations of any mammal, traveling up to 25,000 km annually between feeding and breeding grounds. The population passing KZN — known as IWC Breeding Stock C1 — migrates from Antarctic feeding grounds northward along the east coast of South Africa between May and August, compressed close to shore by the southward-flowing Agulhas Current. They then return southward from October to December, often with newborn calves.

Breeding and calving occur in the warm, sheltered waters off Mozambique, Madagascar, and within the iSimangaliso Wetland Park region. Females give birth to a single calf after a 12-month gestation. Newborns measure 4 to 5 metres and weigh approximately 1,400 kg at birth. Calves nurse on extremely rich milk — containing up to 50% fat — and can gain 45 kg per day during their first months of life.

The bond between mother and calf is exceptionally strong. Mothers are highly protective, often placing themselves between their calf and boats, and the pair remain inseparable for the first year. Calves are weaned at around 11 months but may stay with their mothers for up to two years. Females reach sexual maturity at 4 to 7 years and typically calve every 2 to 3 years.

Conservation Status & Threats

Humpback whales were hunted nearly to extinction during the commercial whaling era of the 19th and 20th centuries. Whalers targeted them relentlessly because they were slow-moving, floated when killed, and yielded enormous quantities of oil, meat, and baleen. By the time international protection was granted in 1966, Southern Hemisphere humpback populations had been reduced to perhaps 10% of their original numbers — from an estimated pre-whaling population of 125,000 to fewer than 10,000 individuals.

The recovery since protection has been remarkable — one of the great conservation success stories of our time. The global population is now estimated at over 135,000 individuals and growing at approximately 7% annually in some regions. The population passing KZN is considered healthy and increasing. Humpbacks were downlisted from Endangered to Least Concern by the IUCN in 2008, though ongoing vigilance is required to ensure continued recovery.

Despite their improved status, humpbacks still face multiple threats including entanglement in fishing gear and shark nets (particularly in KZN waters where shark exclusion nets have caught whales), ship strikes in busy shipping lanes, ocean noise pollution that interferes with communication and navigation, climate change impacts on prey availability in Antarctic feeding grounds, and plastic ingestion. The KwaZulu-Natal Sharks Board and other conservation organizations continue efforts to minimize whale entanglements while maintaining beach safety.

Fascinating Facts

The Longest Fins

Humpback whales possess the longest appendages in the animal kingdom relative to body size. Their pectoral fins can exceed 5 metres in length — nearly one-third of their total body length. The scientific name Megaptera novaeangliae means "giant wings of New England," reflecting these extraordinary fins.

Whale Songs

Male humpbacks produce the most complex and haunting songs in the animal kingdom — vocalizations lasting 10 to 20 minutes that can be repeated continuously for hours. All males in a population sing the same song, which slowly evolves over time. Songs can be heard underwater up to 30 km away and may play a role in mating displays.

Unique Tail Patterns

The black-and-white pigmentation pattern on the underside of each humpback's tail flukes is unique to that individual whale — as distinctive as human fingerprints. Researchers photograph tail flukes to identify and track individual whales across decades, building detailed life histories and migration patterns.

25,000 km Migrations

Humpback whales undertake some of the longest migrations of any mammal on Earth. Some individuals travel over 25,000 km annually between feeding grounds near Antarctica and breeding grounds in tropical waters — an extraordinary journey requiring remarkable endurance and navigation ability.

Bubble-Net Feeding

Humpbacks employ one of the most sophisticated cooperative hunting strategies in the animal kingdom. Groups of whales work together to create cylindrical "nets" of bubbles that concentrate and trap schools of fish. The whales then surge upward through the center with mouths open, engulfing tonnes of prey in a coordinated, spectacular feeding display.

Durban: Whale Heritage Site

Durban and The Bluff became one of the world's first certified Whale Heritage Sites in recognition of the area's exceptional humpback whale viewing opportunities, long history of responsible whale watching, and commitment to cetacean conservation. From June to November, humpbacks pass within metres of the shore — visible from coastal lookouts and during boat-based tours.
