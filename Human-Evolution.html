Human Evolution

The story of human evolution is, at its deepest level, a story intimately connected to water. Africa â€” and KwaZulu-Natal in particular â€” sits at the geographic heart of the human evolutionary narrative. The earliest members of the genus Homo emerged on the African continent approximately 2.8 million years ago, and the anatomically modern humans who would eventually populate every corner of the globe first appeared in Africa around 300,000 years ago. But the connection between human evolution and the aquatic environment runs deeper than geography. A growing body of palaeoanthropological evidence suggests that coastal and lakeside environments played a significant role in shaping key human traits â€” from our upright posture and reduced body hair to our relationship with seafood as a high-quality dietary resource rich in the omega-3 fatty acids and iodine critical for brain development.

KwaZulu-Natal's coastline sits within the broader southern African coastal zone where Middle Stone Age humans lived, fished, harvested shellfish, and left behind some of the oldest evidence of complex symbolic behaviour and systematic marine resource exploitation ever discovered. Sites like Blombos Cave on the Western Cape coast and Pinnacle Point near Mossel Bay demonstrate that anatomically modern humans were using the ocean as a food source and a cultural space well over 160,000 years ago â€” long before the behavioural revolution that enabled our species to disperse globally.

This page traces the major stages of human evolution, with particular attention to the African origins of our lineage and the role of coastal and aquatic environments in shaping who we are as a species whose relationship with the ocean is both ancient and ongoing.
