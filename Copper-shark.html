Copper Shark

Carcharhinus brachyurus ⚠ Vulnerable

Known locally as the bronze whaler, the copper shark is one of the most commonly encountered large shark species along KwaZulu-Natal's coastline. Named for the warm bronzy-copper sheen of their skin, these powerful, streamlined sharks are a defining feature of KZN's marine environment — and one of the most spectacular sights during the annual sardine run, when thousands converge on the baitballs rolling up the coast.

Widely distributed across temperate and subtropical coastal waters, copper sharks occupy a crucial position as apex predators in nearshore ecosystems. Fast, agile, and highly social during feeding events, they are both feared and admired by ocean users — from surfers and swimmers to game fishermen and marine biologists.

Max Length 3.3 m
Max Weight 305 kg
Lifespan ~30 years
Depth Range 0 – 100 m
Maturity Age 13 – 19 years
Litter Size 7 – 24 pups

Physical Description

The copper shark is a large, robust requiem shark with a streamlined, fusiform body built for speed and endurance. Its most distinguishing feature is its colouration — a warm bronze to olive-grey on the dorsal surface that gives the species both its common names: copper shark and bronze whaler. The underside is pale white, with a faint white band along the flanks.

The copper shark closely resembles several other requiem shark species, but can be identified by the combination of its narrow, curved upper teeth with smooth to finely serrated edges, the absence of an interdorsal ridge, and its relatively small first dorsal fin positioned well behind the pectoral fins.

Females are generally larger than males, a trait common among requiem sharks. Large females can approach 3.3 metres in length, while males rarely exceed 2.9 metres. Their streamlined build and powerful, lunate tail make them fast and highly manoeuvrable in the water.

Behaviour

Copper sharks are highly active, fast-swimming sharks that exhibit some of the most dramatic feeding behaviour of any coastal species. During the sardine run — one of the greatest wildlife spectacles on Earth — copper sharks aggregate in their thousands along the KZN coastline to exploit the massive shoals of sardines, working in concert with common dolphins, Cape gannets, and Bryde's whales.

Outside of major feeding events, copper sharks are typically found in loose aggregations segregated by sex and size — a common pattern in requiem sharks. Females and juveniles tend to occupy shallower inshore waters, while larger males range further offshore.

They are considered one of the more dangerous shark species to surfers and swimmers in KZN waters, due to their size, speed, and tendency to investigate objects at the surface. The majority of shark incidents involving copper sharks in South Africa occur in association with recreational water users in the surf zone.

Diet & Feeding

Copper sharks are versatile, highly opportunistic predators. Their diet varies significantly with season, location, and prey availability. During the annual sardine run they feed almost exclusively on sardines, but at other times their diet includes a wide range of fish, cephalopods, and elasmobranchs.

They are capable of cooperative hunting — herding and corralling prey into dense baitballs before attacking at high speed. During such events multiple sharks may feed simultaneously in a highly coordinated manner, a behaviour that maximises feeding efficiency in the presence of abundant prey.

  • Sardines and schooling fish
  • Squid and cuttlefish
  • Rays and small sharks
  • Reef fish and bony fish
  • Occasionally marine mammals

Reproduction

Copper sharks are viviparous, with embryos nourished through a yolk-sac placenta inside the mother's uterus. After a gestation period of approximately 12 months, females give birth to litters of 7 to 24 pups in coastal nursery areas. Newborn pups measure 60 to 70 cm in length.

One of the most notable reproductive characteristics of the copper shark is its exceptionally late maturity — females do not reach sexual maturity until 13 to 19 years of age, and males between 13 and 16 years. This, combined with a gestation period of 12 months and small litter sizes relative to body size, makes the species extremely slow to recover from population decline.

KZN's shallow coastal bays and estuaries serve as important nursery grounds for juvenile copper sharks, which are particularly vulnerable to bycatch in beach seine nets and recreational fishing during their early years.

Habitat & Distribution in KZN

The copper shark has one of the widest distributions of any large coastal shark, found in temperate and subtropical coastal waters across both hemispheres. In South Africa, they are abundant along the entire KwaZulu-Natal coastline, and are one of the most frequently encountered large sharks by divers, fishermen, and beachgoers.

They are typically found in the surf zone and along the continental shelf to depths of around 100 metres, favouring coastal environments with cool to warm temperate water temperatures. In KZN, copper sharks are most abundant during the sardine run season (May to July), when the cold Agulhas countercurrent pushes sardine shoals northward along the coast from the Eastern Cape.

The species is listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List. In South Africa, copper sharks face pressure from targeted commercial and recreational fishing, shark nets deployed along KZN's bathing beaches, and accidental bycatch. The KwaZulu-Natal Sharks Board captures significant numbers of copper sharks in its drumlines and gillnets each year, and ongoing research seeks to develop more selective shark management approaches that reduce bycatch while maintaining public safety.

Fascinating Facts

Sardine Run Star

Copper sharks are one of the primary predators of the annual KZN sardine run — one of the greatest wildlife spectacles on Earth. Thousands of copper sharks converge on the sardine shoals, creating dramatic feeding frenzies visible from the shore and from the air.

Late Developers

Female copper sharks don't reach sexual maturity until up to 19 years of age — one of the latest of any shark species. This extremely slow reproductive timeline makes their populations exceptionally vulnerable to overfishing and very slow to recover.

Bronze Sheen

The distinctive warm bronze or copper colouration of their skin — particularly visible in clear water or when seen from above — is caused by light scattering across their small, close-set dermal denticles, and gives the species both of its common names.

Multi-Species Feeding

During the sardine run, copper sharks feed alongside bottlenose dolphins, common dolphins, Cape gannets, Bryde's whales, and other shark species — one of the rare instances in the ocean where multiple apex predators actively cooperate around the same prey source.

KZN Shark Nets

Copper sharks are one of the most frequently caught species in KZN's beach protection shark nets and drumlines. Ongoing research by the KwaZulu-Natal Sharks Board is focused on developing smarter, more selective shark deterrent technologies to reduce copper shark bycatch.

Coastal Nurseries

The shallow bays and estuaries of KZN serve as critical nursery grounds for juvenile copper sharks. The protection and conservation of these coastal habitats is essential to the long-term survival of copper shark populations in southern African waters.
