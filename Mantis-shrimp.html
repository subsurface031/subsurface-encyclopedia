Mantis Shrimp

Odontodactylus scyllarus & related species ✓ Least Concern

The mantis shrimp is one of the ocean's most extraordinary and misunderstood animals — a predator so uniquely evolved, so physically extreme, and so visually spectacular that it challenges the imagination. Despite their common name, mantis shrimp are neither mantises nor shrimp. They belong to their own ancient order, the Stomatopoda, a lineage that diverged from other crustaceans over 400 million years ago — making them older than the dinosaurs and more distantly related to crabs and lobsters than they might appear. In KwaZulu-Natal, several species inhabit the reefs, sandy flats, and silty substrates from the shallows to moderate depths, where they live as solitary, fiercely territorial ambush predators in burrows of their own excavation.

What makes mantis shrimp genuinely remarkable — beyond their strikingly vivid colouration — is the biological weaponry packed into their second pair of appendages, the raptorial claws. These specialised limbs have evolved along two distinct lines: spearers, which impale soft-bodied prey like fish and shrimp with barbed tips, and smashers, which pulverise hard-shelled prey like crabs, snails, and clams with hammer-like dactyl clubs that accelerate with the same force as a bullet. The smashing strike of a large mantis shrimp is one of the fastest and most powerful movements in the biological world — capable of shattering aquarium glass and delivering a blow that can break a human finger.

Max Length ~38 cm
Strike Speed ~80 km/h
Strike Force ~1500 N
Vision 16 colour channels
Lifespan ~20 years
Habitat 1 – 70 m

Physical Description

Mantis shrimp have an elongated, somewhat flattened body divided into the head and thorax — fused into a cephalothorax covered by a hard carapace — and a broad, segmented abdomen that terminates in a distinctive fan-shaped tail. The body is remarkably flexible compared to most crustaceans, and the abdomen can curl powerfully to deliver a tail-flip escape response. The carapace covers only the front portion of the thorax, leaving the rear thoracic segments and their associated appendages exposed and mobile — a key structural feature that allows the wide range of motion needed for the raptorial strike.

The most visually striking feature of many mantis shrimp species — particularly the peacock mantis shrimp (Odontodactylus scyllarus) found on KZN's coral and rocky reefs — is their extraordinary colouration. The carapace, abdomen, and tail bear vivid patterns in electric green, orange, red, blue, and purple, with iridescent highlights that shift with the angle of light. This colouration is not merely decorative — it plays roles in species recognition, mate attraction, and territorial signalling, with mantis shrimp able to perceive fluorescent and polarised light signals invisible to most other animals.

The eyes are mounted on independent, freely moveable stalks and are among the most complex visual organs in the animal kingdom. Each eye is divided into three regions that together can perceive wavelengths from deep ultraviolet through visible light to far infrared, as well as multiple forms of polarised light. The eyes move independently of each other and can rotate in three planes simultaneously — allowing a mantis shrimp to track multiple targets or examine an object from different angles without moving its body.

Behaviour

Mantis shrimp are among the most aggressively territorial of all marine crustaceans. Each individual occupies a burrow — excavated in sand, mud, or coral rubble, or appropriated from other animals — that it defends vigorously against all intruders, including conspecifics. The burrow is a critical resource: it provides protection from predators, a stable microhabitat, and a base from which the animal sallies out to hunt or ambush passing prey. Territorial disputes are resolved through ritualised combat in which opponents display their raptorial appendages and deliver controlled strikes — a remarkable example of conflict resolution that avoids lethal injury between members of the same species.

Despite their solitary and aggressive nature outside the breeding season, mantis shrimp form monogamous pair bonds that can persist for many years — a highly unusual social arrangement among crustaceans. Mated pairs share burrows, jointly guard egg masses, and engage in complex social interactions including mutual grooming and coordinated defence. Some species in the smashing group are among the most faithful pair-bonders of any marine invertebrate, with couples remaining together for 20 years or more and raising multiple clutches of eggs together over their lifetimes.

The hunting behaviour of mantis shrimp is highly variable and sophisticated. Spearing species typically wait motionless at the burrow entrance for suitable prey to approach within striking range — then launch the raptorial appendages in a movement almost too fast for the human eye to follow, impaling the prey on barbed spines. Smashing species actively hunt across the substrate, searching for hard-shelled prey that they then stun, break open, and consume. Both types have been observed learning the patrol routes and habits of prey items and positioning themselves to intercept them — a level of predatory intelligence rarely attributed to crustaceans.

Diet & Feeding

The diet of mantis shrimp is strongly linked to the type of raptorial appendage they possess. Spearing mantis shrimp — represented in KZN by several species inhabiting sandy and silty substrates — feed primarily on soft-bodied prey that can be impaled and held: fish, shrimp, squid, and polychaete worms. Smashing species target hard-shelled prey that their dactyl clubs can crack or shatter: crabs, snails, hermit crabs, clams, and other bivalves. Both groups are capable of consuming prey that is remarkably large relative to their own body size, and they will sometimes tackle items almost as large as themselves.

The smashing strike is a technological marvel of biological engineering. The dactyl clubs of smashing mantis shrimp are constructed from a helical arrangement of mineral fibres that distributes the enormous impact forces and resists cracking — a structure so effective that materials scientists have studied it as inspiration for better composite materials in helmets and vehicle armour. The strike is powered not by muscle alone but by a spring-and-latch mechanism that stores energy and releases it in a single explosive movement — generating accelerations of up to 10,000 G and producing cavitation bubbles in the water that collapse with additional force even if the initial strike misses.

  • Fish, shrimp, and squid (spearing species)
  • Crabs, hermit crabs, and other crustaceans
  • Snails, limpets, and hard-shelled molluscs
  • Clams, oysters, and bivalves
  • Polychaete worms and soft invertebrates
  • Occasionally octopuses and slow-moving fish

Reproduction & Life History

Reproduction in mantis shrimp involves elaborate courtship displays in which both partners exhibit their colouration and perform ritualised behaviours including fluorescent signalling visible only to others with mantis shrimp vision. Mating can occur at any time of year in KZN's warm subtropical waters, though there may be seasonal peaks associated with water temperature and prey availability. Fertilisation is internal, and females deposit their eggs into compact, sticky masses that they carry in their thoracic appendages or store in the burrow.

Females brood their eggs with remarkable dedication — aerating them continuously with fanning movements of the abdomen, turning and cleaning them to prevent fungal infection, and defending the burrow aggressively against any approaching threat. In some species, both parents participate in guarding the egg mass. Incubation lasts 2 to 4 weeks depending on temperature, after which the eggs hatch into free-swimming larvae that join the plankton. Larval development is prolonged — lasting 3 months or more through multiple stages — before juveniles settle onto the substrate and begin their benthic lives.

Juvenile mantis shrimp are miniature replicas of the adults, already equipped with functional raptorial appendages, and are immediately territorial and predatory. Growth rates are moderate, with most species reaching sexual maturity within 1 to 2 years. Mantis shrimp are surprisingly long-lived for crustaceans — up to 20 years in some species — and the commitment to long-term pair bonds and repeated breeding over many years makes them unusual among marine invertebrates in their complex social and reproductive behaviour.

Mantis Shrimp in KZN Waters & Their Ecological Role

Several species of mantis shrimp inhabit KwaZulu-Natal's diverse coastal environments. The peacock mantis shrimp (Odontodactylus scyllarus) — the largest and most visually spectacular — is a smasher found on coral and rocky reefs, particularly in the warm, clear waters of the northern KZN coast around Sodwana Bay and the iSimangaliso Wetland Park. Smaller spearing species are widespread in sandy and silty habitats throughout the coast, including estuaries and sheltered bays, where they excavate burrows in soft sediments and ambush passing shrimp and small fish. Most species are cryptic and nocturnal, making them far more common than casual observation would suggest.

Divers and snorkellers who encounter mantis shrimp in KZN are frequently startled by the speed and ferocity of their defensive response. A mantis shrimp spotted foraging in the open on a Sodwana reef may retreat rapidly to a nearby crevice and disappear — but if cornered or approached too closely, the response is a lightning-fast defensive strike. Experienced divers treat them with considerable respect: the term "thumb-splitter" is widely used among marine aquarists and divers who have learned the hard way about the consequences of careless handling. Even through a 3mm neoprene glove, the strike of a large peacock mantis shrimp can cause a fracture.

Ecologically, mantis shrimp are important mid-level predators in the reef and sandy habitat communities of KZN. As smashers, they are keystone predators of hard-shelled invertebrates — controlling populations of crabs, snails, and bivalves that could otherwise become overabundant and alter habitat structure. As spearers, they are significant predators of small fish and shrimp in sandy habitats, competing with and complementing other burrow-dwelling predators. Their burrows provide habitat for commensal species including small crabs, shrimps, and polychaete worms that shelter in the excavated tunnels.

The extraordinary visual system of mantis shrimp has become a topic of significant scientific interest, with researchers studying their ability to detect polarised light for potential applications in cancer detection, data encryption, and materials science. Their weapon system — the dactyl club with its impact-resistant helicoidal fibre structure — has inspired new approaches to lightweight composite armour. Mantis shrimp serve as an important reminder that KZN's reefs harbour animals whose biological innovations dwarf anything human engineers have yet achieved, and that protecting these ecosystems is not merely an ecological imperative but a scientific one as well.

Fascinating Facts

The Fastest Strike in the Sea

The striking appendage of a smashing mantis shrimp accelerates at over 10,000 G — roughly the same as a bullet leaving a gun barrel. The entire strike from initiation to contact takes less than 3 milliseconds — far too fast for the human eye to see. This extraordinary speed is achieved not by muscle alone but by a biological spring-and-latch mechanism that stores energy and releases it instantaneously, similar in principle to the mechanism of a crossbow.

Cavitation Bubbles

The mantis shrimp's strike is doubly lethal: not only does the dactyl club deliver a devastating direct impact, but the speed of the strike creates cavitation bubbles in the surrounding water — regions of near-vacuum that collapse almost instantly with a secondary shockwave. Even if the initial strike misses, the collapsing cavitation bubbles deliver enough force to stun or injure prey. This means a mantis shrimp effectively hits its target twice with every strike.

16 Types of Colour Receptors

Humans have three types of colour photoreceptors — red, green, and blue. Mantis shrimp have sixteen, covering a range from deep ultraviolet to far infrared, plus the ability to detect multiple forms of circularly and linearly polarised light. Paradoxically, despite this extraordinary visual hardware, research suggests mantis shrimp may not actually distinguish colours better than humans — instead, they may use their photoreceptors as a rapid colour-identification system that doesn't require the brain to compare wavelengths.

Monogamous for Life

Smashing mantis shrimp species are among the most devoted long-term partners in the ocean. Mated pairs share burrows, jointly brood and guard eggs, and have been documented remaining together for over 20 years — an extraordinary commitment for an animal with a lifespan rarely exceeding that figure. The pair bond is maintained through complex visual signalling using fluorescent patterns on the body that are unique to individuals and visible only to other mantis shrimp.

Aquarium Breakers

Mantis shrimp are notorious in the marine aquarium hobby for their ability to shatter glass aquarium panels. A large peacock mantis shrimp striking repeatedly at the same point on standard 6mm aquarium glass can break through completely — a fact discovered by many hobbyists who underestimated the force packed into a few centimetres of armoured crustacean. Mantis shrimp require specially reinforced acrylic tanks and are generally considered one of the most challenging and demanding animals in the marine aquarium trade.

Ancient Lineage

Stomatopods — the order to which all mantis shrimp belong — have been prowling the world's oceans for over 400 million years, predating the dinosaurs by more than 200 million years and surviving multiple mass extinction events that wiped out most complex animal life. The basic body plan and raptorial appendage design have remained remarkably consistent throughout this vast time span, making the mantis shrimp one of the most evolutionarily stable and successful predator designs in the history of life on Earth.
