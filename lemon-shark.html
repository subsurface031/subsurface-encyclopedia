Negaprion brevirostris — Lemon Shark, KZN

Lemon Shark

Negaprion brevirostris ⚠ Vulnerable

Named for their distinctive yellowish-brown colouration, lemon sharks are medium to large coastal sharks that inhabit the shallow subtropical and tropical waters of the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans, as well as the warm inshore environments of KwaZulu-Natal. Stocky, powerful, and highly adapted for life in shallow coastal habitats, lemon sharks are one of the most extensively studied shark species in the world.

Their tolerance for low-oxygen, murky, and brackish water environments — including mangroves, coral reefs, and river mouths — makes them uniquely suited to the coastal habitats of northern KZN. They are known for their curious and sometimes bold behaviour, yet are generally considered non-aggressive toward humans unless provoked.

Max Length 3.4 m
Max Weight 183 kg
Lifespan ~27 years
Depth Range 0 – 92 m
Maturity Age 12 – 16 years
Litter Size 4 – 17 pups

Physical Description

The lemon shark is a robust, broad-headed shark with a short, blunt snout and two dorsal fins of nearly equal size — a feature that readily distinguishes it from most other requiem shark species. The second dorsal fin is unusually large, almost matching the first in height, giving the shark a distinctive silhouette in the water.

Their colouration is a uniform yellowish-brown to olive-grey on the dorsal surface, fading to a pale yellow or white underside. This yellowish tint — from which the species derives its common name — provides effective camouflage against the sandy, sun-dappled seafloors of their preferred shallow-water habitats.

They have small eyes with a nictitating membrane — a translucent third eyelid that protects the eye during feeding — and relatively small, narrow teeth adapted for gripping slippery fish prey.

Behaviour

Lemon sharks are one of the most socially complex shark species studied. Research has revealed that they form consistent social groups based on size and sex, and that individuals demonstrate clear preferences for associating with certain other sharks — a level of social organisation rarely observed in elasmobranchs.

They are largely nocturnal, becoming most active at night when they move into shallower water to hunt. During the day they are often found resting on the seafloor, sometimes in loose aggregations. They can remain stationary for extended periods, relying on buccal pumping to move water over their gills rather than needing to swim continuously.

Lemon sharks show strong site fidelity, returning repeatedly to the same reef areas, mangrove nurseries, and feeding grounds. Long-term studies have tracked individual sharks to the same locations over periods of more than a decade.

Diet & Feeding

Lemon sharks are opportunistic predators that feed primarily on bony fish, making use of their highly developed electroreceptive and olfactory senses to locate prey in murky, low-visibility coastal waters. They are particularly well suited to hunting in the complex, root-filled environments of mangrove systems, where many fish species seek refuge.

Unlike faster, more pelagic sharks, lemon sharks rely on stealth and ambush rather than speed — slowly approaching prey before striking with a rapid lunge. Their relatively blunt teeth are effective for gripping and crushing rather than slicing, reflecting their diet of whole fish and crustaceans.

  • Bony fish (mullet, catfish, jacks)
  • Rays and skates
  • Squid and octopus
  • Crustaceans and molluscs
  • Seabirds (occasionally)

Reproduction

Lemon sharks are viviparous, with embryos nourished through a placental connection to the mother. After a gestation period of 10 to 12 months, females give birth to litters of 4 to 17 pups in shallow mangrove nursery habitats. These nursery areas are critical — pups remain in the same mangrove system for several years, using the complex root structures for shelter and the abundant small fish for food.

Remarkably, long-term research in the Bahamas has shown that female lemon sharks return to their own birth site to give birth — a behaviour known as natal philopatry. This extraordinary site fidelity means that the loss of a single nursery habitat can have cascading, multi-generational effects on local populations.

Females reproduce every other year, and their late maturity age of 12 to 16 years means populations are exceptionally slow to recover from overfishing or habitat loss.

Habitat & Distribution in KZN

Lemon sharks are most commonly associated with the shallow, warm coastal waters of the northern KwaZulu-Natal coast, particularly in and around the mangrove and estuarine habitats of the iSimangaliso Wetland Park. These environments — characterised by warm, nutrient-rich, often murky water — closely resemble the Atlantic mangrove systems where lemon sharks have been most extensively studied.

They are found in a wide variety of inshore habitats including coral reefs, sandy flats, mangrove edges, river mouths, and enclosed bays. Their tolerance for reduced salinity allows them to venture into estuarine and occasionally freshwater environments, though they are primarily marine animals.

Globally, lemon sharks are distributed across the tropical and subtropical Atlantic Ocean, with a separate population in the eastern Pacific. Their occurrence in KZN waters represents the southern extent of their Indian Ocean range. They are listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List, facing threats from targeted fishing, the shark fin trade, and the widespread destruction of mangrove nursery habitats — one of the most threatened ecosystems on Earth.

Fascinating Facts

Social Sharks

Lemon sharks are among the most socially complex sharks known to science. Studies have shown they form stable social groups, demonstrate individual "friendships," and actively choose to associate with familiar sharks — challenging the notion of sharks as purely solitary animals.

Birth Site Loyalty

Female lemon sharks return to their own birthplace to give birth — a phenomenon called natal philopatry. Individual females have been documented returning to the same mangrove nursery year after year across their entire reproductive lives.

Research Icons

Lemon sharks are one of the most studied shark species in the world. Long-term research programmes, particularly in Bimini, Bahamas, have tracked individual sharks for over 20 years, transforming our understanding of shark intelligence, social behaviour, and learning.

Can Learn by Observation

Laboratory and field studies have demonstrated that lemon sharks are capable of social learning — observing and copying the behaviour of other sharks to solve problems and locate food. This cognitive ability was previously thought to be limited to birds and mammals.

Stationary Breathing

Unlike many sharks that must swim constantly to breathe, lemon sharks can pump water over their gills using buccal pumping while remaining completely still on the seafloor — allowing them to rest and conserve energy during periods of inactivity.

Equal Dorsal Fins

One of the lemon shark's most distinctive physical traits is its two nearly equal-sized dorsal fins — unusually large second dorsal fins compared to other requiem sharks. This feature makes them easy to identify from above or at the surface.
