Barnacle

Chthamalus, Tetraclita & Balanus species ✓ Least Concern

The barnacle is arguably the most misunderstood animal on KwaZulu-Natal's rocky shores — routinely mistaken for a mollusc, dismissed as mere encrustation, and walked over by millions of beachgoers who have no idea that beneath each grey-white volcanic cone lives a crustacean that is, in the words of the Victorian naturalist Louis Agassiz, "nothing more than a little shrimp-like animal, standing on its head in a limestone house and kicking food into its mouth with its feet." This description, though charmingly reductive, captures something essentially true about barnacles: they are true crustaceans — more closely related to crabs and lobsters than to the limpets and periwinkles they resemble — that have evolved one of the most spectacularly unusual lifestyles in the animal kingdom.

Barnacles dominate the upper intertidal zone on KZN's rocky shores with a completeness that is nothing short of extraordinary. On suitable surfaces — hard rock, concrete harbour walls, wooden pilings, the hulls of boats, and even the skin of whales and sea turtles — barnacles form dense, continuous colonies of interlocking shells that can cover every square centimetre of available hard substrate. Their ability to survive prolonged aerial exposure during low tide, withstand temperature extremes, and continue feeding efficiently when submerged has made them the undisputed masters of one of the harshest habitats in the coastal zone — the zone where land and sea meet and where the physical environment changes radically every few hours.

Physical Description

The familiar cone-shaped form of an acorn barnacle — the most common type found on KZN's rocky shores — is not actually a shell in the mollusc sense but a fortress constructed from multiple calcified plates that the barnacle secretes and grows throughout its life. The outer wall consists of four to six lateral plates fused or interlocked to form a rigid circular or oval wall. The top of the structure is covered by two pairs of moveable opercular plates — the tergum and scutum — that open to allow the feeding appendages to extend during submersion and close tightly during aerial exposure to retain moisture and prevent desiccation.

Inside this calcium carbonate fortress, the barnacle lives permanently cemented head-down to the substrate — a posture it adopted at the moment of permanent settlement as a cypris larva and never changed. The body within is shrimp-like in organisation, with a thorax bearing six pairs of biramous appendages — the cirri — that are the feeding organs. When submerged and actively feeding, the opercular plates part and the cirri are extended and swept rhythmically through the water in a net-like motion, collecting suspended particles. When the cirri are withdrawn and the opercular plates closed, the barnacle is effectively sealed from the outside environment — retaining a small pocket of water around the body that sustains it through hours of tidal exposure.

Several species of acorn barnacle are found in KZN's intertidal zone, occupying distinct height bands that reflect their differing tolerances to aerial exposure. Chthamalus species dominate the upper shore where exposure periods are longest, while larger Tetraclita and Balanus species occur lower on the shore where submersion is more frequent. Goose barnacles — a different family with a flexible stalk rather than a cemented base — are occasionally found attached to flotsam washed ashore on KZN's beaches, having drifted in from offshore where they attach to floating objects.

Behaviour

For sedentary, permanently fixed animals, barnacles display a surprisingly sophisticated range of behaviours. Feeding is the most visually apparent — the rhythmic extension and retraction of the feathery cirri through the opened operculum is a characteristic sight on any submerged rocky surface, the paired plates parting and the fan of feeding legs sweeping the water in coordinated beats that can number several per second when plankton concentrations are high. The sweep pattern and cirri beat rate are adjusted dynamically in response to current speed and food particle concentration — faster currents require faster beats, and the direction of sweeping aligns with prevailing water flow to maximise particle capture efficiency.

Despite their cemented lifestyle, barnacles are not entirely passive. When disturbed — by the shadow of a predator, a sudden wave impact, or physical contact — the cirri are withdrawn instantaneously and the opercular plates snap shut with a speed and force that can surprise first-time observers. This closure response is triggered by vibration and pressure receptors in the body wall and is controlled by fast-conducting nerve pathways that prioritise speed over precision. Barnacles can also respond to chemical cues in the water — increasing cirri beat rate in the presence of food-related chemicals and reducing or halting feeding in the presence of predator-related chemicals such as those released by attacking whelks.

Settlement behaviour in barnacle larvae — the cypris stage — is arguably the most consequential behaviour in the barnacle's entire life. Cypris larvae spend days to weeks searching for an appropriate settlement site, testing surfaces with specialised attachment appendages and chemoreceptors before committing to permanent cementation. The chemical signal most powerfully triggering settlement in most barnacle species is the presence of the cement protein of already-settled barnacles — meaning that cypris larvae preferentially settle near existing barnacle colonies, generating the dense aggregations that characterise barnacle-dominated intertidal surfaces.

Diet & Feeding

Barnacles are suspension feeders — entirely dependent on particles suspended in the water column that passes over their rocky shore habitat during tidal submersion. The cirri — modified legs — function as a dynamic filter, sweeping through the water to collect phytoplankton cells, bacteria, detritus particles, and zooplankton small enough to be captured by the setae (fine hairs) that fringe each cirrus. Captured particles are transferred to the mouthparts and ingested. The efficiency of this feeding system depends on the concentration of food particles in the water, current speed, and the barnacle's own cirri beat rate and sweep pattern.

During the brief windows of tidal submersion, barnacles feed as intensively as conditions allow — maximising energy intake to fuel growth, reproduction, and the metabolic costs of maintaining the body during the much longer periods of aerial exposure when feeding is impossible. A barnacle in the upper intertidal may be submerged for only a few hours per tidal cycle, giving it a very narrow feeding window that requires maximum efficiency when the opportunity arises. Despite these constraints, high-shore barnacles can achieve growth rates and reproductive outputs comparable to lower-shore species with much longer submersion periods — a testament to the efficiency of suspension feeding even in brief windows.

  • Phytoplankton — diatoms and dinoflagellates
  • Bacteria and picoplankton
  • Suspended organic detritus and particles
  • Zooplankton nauplii and copepod larvae
  • Dissolved organic compounds absorbed through body surface
  • Microbial films during brief contact with substrate water

Reproduction & Life History

Barnacle reproduction involves a fascinating solution to the challenge of mating while permanently cemented to a substrate — they are hermaphrodites, with each individual possessing both male and female reproductive organs, and they mate by extending a remarkably long, flexible penis to reach and fertilise neighbouring individuals. The barnacle penis is, relative to body size, the longest of any animal — sometimes extending up to eight times the body length — allowing fertilisation of multiple neighbours from a single fixed position. In dense barnacle aggregations, virtually every individual can potentially mate with every immediate neighbour, maximising reproductive success within the constraints of immobility.

Fertilised eggs are brooded within the mantle cavity through winter and spring, hatching into nauplius larvae that are released into the water column. Barnacle nauplii are a significant component of coastal plankton in KZN, providing food for planktivorous fish, bivalves, and other filter feeders. The larvae pass through six nauplius stages over 2 to 6 weeks before metamorphosing into the non-feeding cypris larva — the settlement stage. The cypris larva has a finite energy reserve that must sustain it through settlement site searching, and delays in finding a suitable site progressively reduce settlement success and post-settlement survival.

Once cemented, a barnacle grows by adding new material to the edges of its calcareous plates — the plates do not moult as in other crustaceans, though the internal soft parts are moulted periodically. Growth rates are highest in juveniles in optimal conditions and slow progressively with age and increasing size. Barnacles can reach sexual maturity within a few months of settlement in warm KZN waters. Lifespan varies by species and shore position — upper-shore species exposed to greater physical stress tend to be shorter-lived at 2 to 3 years, while lower-shore and subtidal species may live for 5 to 10 years or more.

Barnacles in KZN: Rocky Shore Ecology & Biofouling

Barnacles are the structural foundation of KwaZulu-Natal's upper intertidal community — the primary space occupiers of the zone between the high-tide mark and the mid-shore mussel belt that characterises rocky shores throughout the KZN coast. The dense barnacle crusts that cover upper-shore rock surfaces are not merely passive substrate — they create a complex three-dimensional microhabitat that shelters dozens of associated species. The crevices between adjacent barnacle shells, the spaces beneath loose shells, and the accumulated organic matter within barnacle colonies provide refuge and feeding habitat for small polychaete worms, isopods, amphipods, mites, and the larvae of larger intertidal invertebrates. Remove the barnacles and the structural habitat they create, and a substantial fraction of the associated community disappears with them.

Barnacles are important prey for a range of intertidal predators in KZN. Whelks — gastropod snails of the family Muricidae — are the most significant barnacle predators on most KZN shores, drilling through the opercular plates with a specialised rasping organ (the radula) and consuming the soft body within. The predation patterns of whelks on barnacle colonies are often clearly visible as patches of empty, drilled barnacle shells surrounded by intact living individuals. Oystercatchers use their powerful bills to chisel barnacles from the rock surface and are major predators of barnacle colonies in the mid-shore. At high tide, small fish including klipfish and blennies pick individual barnacles from the rock surface, while shorebirds including turnstones systematically work barnacle-covered surfaces at low tide.

The settlement and growth of barnacles on artificial hard surfaces — boat hulls, harbour walls, jetty pilings, buoys, and underwater structures — creates significant economic costs through increased drag on vessels and accelerated corrosion of metal surfaces. Marine biofouling by barnacles costs the global shipping industry billions of dollars annually in fuel and maintenance, and the study of barnacle settlement and adhesion — with the goal of developing effective antifouling systems — has been one of the most active areas of applied marine biology for over a century. KZN's warm, productive coastal waters support particularly rapid barnacle settlement on artificial surfaces, and the fouling communities that develop on harbour structures in Durban, Richards Bay, and other ports within months of any cleaning provide a vivid demonstration of barnacle colonisation capacity.

Paradoxically, the same extraordinary adhesion that makes barnacles such costly fouling organisms has attracted intense scientific and engineering interest in barnacle cement as a model for new underwater adhesives. Barnacle cement is a complex protein-based material that bonds to virtually any hard surface — clean rock, fouled metal, polished glass, living tissue — while submerged in seawater, curing to a strength of approximately 50 kilograms per square centimetre. No human-made underwater adhesive approaches this performance across such a broad range of substrates and conditions, and understanding the molecular mechanisms of barnacle cement is an active research area with potential applications in medicine, dentistry, and materials engineering.

Fascinating Facts

Standing on Their Heads

The zoologist Louis Agassiz famously described barnacles as animals that spend their lives "standing on their heads" — and this is anatomically accurate. A barnacle cements the top of its head to the substrate at settlement and never moves again. The cirri it uses for feeding are modified legs, meaning that a barnacle quite literally kicks food into its mouth. This radical reorientation of the body plan relative to ancestral crustaceans makes barnacles one of the most anatomically unusual animals in the intertidal zone.

Record-Breaking Adhesion

Barnacle cement bonds to wet surfaces with a tensile strength of approximately 50 kilograms per square centimetre — roughly five times stronger than most commercial epoxy adhesives when applied underwater. A barnacle colony covering one square metre of rock is held in place with a combined force of several tonnes. This extraordinary adhesion persists indefinitely in seawater and resists wave forces, abrasion, biological degradation, and the attempts of predators and boat-cleaners alike to dislodge it. Understanding this cement is a genuine frontier of materials science.

The Longest Penis (Relative to Body Size)

To solve the mating problem of being permanently cemented to a rock surrounded by equally immobile neighbours, barnacles evolved a penis that can extend up to eight times the length of their body — proportionally the longest penis of any animal on Earth. This flexible organ is extended through the partially opened opercular plates to reach and fertilise neighbouring individuals. In dense colonies, the searching, probing movements of extended barnacle penises are clearly visible under magnification on a submerged barnacle rock surface.

Zonation Engineers

The distinct horizontal banding of different barnacle species on KZN's rocky shores — different species occupying precisely defined height zones between low and high tide marks — is one of the clearest examples of ecological zonation in the natural world. Each species band reflects a precise balance between the barnacle's physiological tolerance for aerial exposure and desiccation and its competitive ability against other barnacle species lower on the shore. This zonation pattern, studied extensively on South African shores, has provided fundamental insights into how physical stress and biological competition together structure intertidal communities.

Whale Passengers

Several barnacle species are obligate commensals of cetaceans — found nowhere else except attached to the skin of whales and dolphins. Humpback whales, southern right whales, and other large cetaceans that visit KZN's waters carry dense colonies of specialised barnacles on their heads, flukes, and flippers. These whale barnacles travel with their hosts across ocean basins, feeding on the plankton-rich water that passes over the whale's body in motion. The pattern and distribution of barnacle colonies on individual whales can be used by researchers to identify specific animals across years and ocean basins.

The Biofouling Problem

Barnacle settlement on the hulls of vessels increases drag by up to 60 percent, dramatically increasing fuel consumption and carbon emissions. The global shipping industry spends an estimated USD 10 billion annually managing marine biofouling — dominated by barnacles. In KZN's warm, productive waters, a vessel left unprotected at anchor in Durban harbour can accumulate a visible barnacle crust within days and a mature fouling community within weeks. The search for effective, environmentally acceptable antifouling solutions — to replace the highly toxic tin-based compounds banned in the early 2000s — remains one of the most economically important challenges in marine science.
