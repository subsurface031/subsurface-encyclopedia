Endangered Species & SASSi Listings

KwaZulu-Natal's coastal and aquatic ecosystems are home to extraordinary biodiversity — but many of the species profiled in this encyclopedia face serious, ongoing threats from overfishing, habitat destruction, pollution, climate change, and bycatch. Understanding which species are under pressure, and why, is the first step toward making choices that support their survival. This page brings together the conservation status of featured species using both the global IUCN Red List framework and the South African Sustainable Seafood Initiative (SASSi) consumer guide — the two most widely recognised tools for communicating species conservation status in South Africa.

The IUCN Red List assesses species against global population trends and extinction risk, assigning categories from Least Concern through to Extinct. SASSi, operated by WWF South Africa, focuses specifically on the sustainability of South African fisheries — rating commonly consumed seafood as green (best choice), orange (think twice), or red (avoid) based on stock health, fishing method impacts, bycatch levels, and management effectiveness. These two systems sometimes assign different urgency to the same species, reflecting different geographic scopes and assessment criteria — but together they give the most complete conservation picture available to a KZN consumer or ocean enthusiast.

We encourage all users of this encyclopedia — recreational fishers, divers, restaurant-goers, or simply curious coastal visitors — to use this information to make informed decisions. Every choice to avoid a red-listed species, to select a sustainable seafood alternative, or to report suspected illegal harvesting is a genuine contribution to the conservation of KZN's remarkable aquatic heritage.

SASSi Consumer Guide — Colour Key

Green — Best Choice

Well-managed species with healthy populations and minimal environmental impact. Recommended as a sustainable seafood choice.

Orange — Think Twice

Some concerns around stock health, management, or fishing method impacts. Consider purchasing less frequently or sourcing from certified-sustainable suppliers.

Red — Avoid

Overfished, poorly managed, or fishing method causes unacceptable environmental damage. SASSi recommends consumers avoid purchasing entirely.

Species of Greatest Concern

SASSi: Avoid

Rock Lobster (Crayfish)

Panulirus homarus

Rock lobster populations along the KZN coast have declined dramatically over recent decades due to sustained overexploitation through recreational and commercial harvesting. Illegal night-diving outside permitted seasons continues to put severe pressure on remaining stocks. SASSi rates east coast rock lobster as red — stocks have not recovered sufficiently to support current harvest levels.

Overfishing Illegal harvesting Slow reproduction Climate change

IUCN: Critically Endangered

Scalloped Hammerhead Shark

Sphyrna lewini

Once abundant in KZN's coastal waters and a fixture of the Sardine Run, scalloped hammerheads have declined catastrophically. Highly valued in the shark fin trade and vulnerable due to their schooling behaviour, the species is now Critically Endangered globally. KZN shark nets incidentally catch hammerheads, adding further pressure to already severely depleted populations.

Fin trade Shark nets Schooling vulnerability Slow reproduction

IUCN: Endangered

Loggerhead Sea Turtle

Caretta caretta

Loggerhead sea turtles nest on KZN's northern beaches — particularly in the iSimangaliso Wetland Park — in one of the most significant nesting aggregations in the southern hemisphere. Despite decades of protection, turtles remain threatened by entanglement in fishing gear, marine debris ingestion, artificial lighting disorienting hatchlings, and coastal development encroaching on nesting beaches.

Prawn (Penaeid Shrimp)

Penaeus indicus & Metapenaeus monoceros

KZN's commercial prawn trawl fishery operates under management controls but remains concerning due to high bycatch ratios — multiple kilograms of non-target marine life discarded for every kilogram of prawn landed. Estuarine nursery habitat degradation further threatens recruitment success. Consider farmed or trap-caught alternatives where available.

Trawl bycatch Estuarine degradation Habitat loss

SASSi: Think Twice

Mantis Shrimp

Odontodactylus & Gonodactylus spp.

Mantis shrimp are not heavily targeted in KZN but are frequently taken as bycatch in prawn trawls and occasionally collected from tidal areas for the aquarium and local seafood trade. Their dependence on healthy sandy and reef habitats makes them sensitive to trawling disturbance and coastal development. SASSi guidance defaults to orange for trawl-associated species in the region.

Trawl bycatch Aquarium trade Habitat sensitivity

IUCN: Endangered

Whale Shark

Rhincodon typus

Whale sharks are occasional visitors to KZN's warm coastal waters and are fully protected under South African law. Globally, populations have declined substantially due to targeted fishing in parts of Asia and vessel strike. Their extremely slow maturation — estimated at 25 to 30 years to sexual maturity — and low reproductive rate make recovery from depletion exceptionally slow.

Vessel strike Targeted fishing Slow maturation

Species With Stable or Least Concern Status

IUCN: Least Concern

Ghost Crab

Ocypode cursor & O. ceratophthalmus

Ghost crab populations on KZN's beaches remain relatively healthy. Beach vehicle traffic, artificial lighting, and human disturbance during nesting season cause localised impacts. Their wide range and high reproductive output provide resilience. Protection of undisturbed beach habitat is the most important conservation measure for this species.

Beach vehicles Light pollution Human disturbance

IUCN: Least Concern

Mud Prawn (Upogebia)

Upogebia africana

Mud prawns are Least Concern globally but heavily exploited as fishing bait in KZN estuaries, with documented population depletion in heavily accessed systems. Their slow reproductive rate and burrow-dependent lifestyle mean local population recovery from intensive harvesting is very slow. Sustainable bait harvesting regulations are a critical conservation measure.

Bait harvesting Estuarine degradation Slow recovery

IUCN: Least Concern

Swimming Crab (Blue Crab)

Portunus pelagicus

Blue swimming crab populations in KZN are currently stable, supported by fast growth, high fecundity, and productive estuarine nursery habitats. Primary concerns are estuarine habitat degradation and incidental capture of juveniles in prawn trawl bycatch. Recreational drop-netting is low-impact when bag limits are respected.

Estuarine habitat loss Trawl bycatch

IUCN: Least Concern

Hermit Crab

Clibanarius virescens & related spp.

Hermit crabs are abundant on KZN's rocky shores and reefs. The collection of gastropod shells for the decorative trade is reducing availability of the borrowed shells they depend on — particularly in high-tourism areas. Tidal pool disturbance and collection for the aquarium trade are secondary concerns. Overall IUCN status remains Least Concern.

Shell collection Tidal pool disturbance
Full Species Profile
IUCN: Least Concern

Fiddler Crab

Uca annulipes & related spp.

Fiddler crabs are currently Least Concern globally, but their dependence on healthy mangrove and estuarine habitats makes them sensitive indicators of ecosystem condition. Mangrove loss and estuarine sedimentation from upstream development are the primary indirect threats in KZN. Population density directly proxies mangrove ecosystem health throughout the coast.

Mangrove loss Estuarine sedimentation Pollution

IUCN: Least Concern

Barnacle

Chthamalus, Tetraclita & Balanus spp.

Barnacles are abundant, highly resilient, and face no direct exploitation pressure in KZN. Primary threats are indirect — oil spills and chemical pollution can rapidly kill intertidal colonies, and ocean acidification may affect calcification over longer timescales. Rocky shore habitat loss from coastal infrastructure development is a localised concern in KZN.

Oil spills Ocean acidification Shore development

About SASSi & The IUCN Red List

The South African Sustainable Seafood Initiative (SASSi) is operated by WWF South Africa and provides consumer-facing guidance on the sustainability of seafood sold and consumed in South Africa. SASSi ratings are based on comprehensive stock assessments, fishing method impact analyses, management effectiveness evaluations, and bycatch data — updated regularly as new information becomes available. The free SASSi app and website allow consumers to check the rating of any seafood species before purchasing. SASSi also works directly with the fishing industry and retailers to promote sourcing of certified-sustainable alternatives.

The IUCN Red List of Threatened Species is the world's most comprehensive inventory of the global conservation status of biological species. Maintained by the International Union for Conservation of Nature, the Red List uses rigorous scientific criteria to evaluate extinction risk across nine categories: Extinct (EX), Extinct in the Wild (EW), Critically Endangered (CR), Endangered (EN), Vulnerable (VU), Near Threatened (NT), Least Concern (LC), Data Deficient (DD), and Not Evaluated (NE). IUCN assessments are global in scope and may not fully reflect local or regional population conditions — a species that is Least Concern globally may face significant pressures in specific locations such as KZN.

The conservation status information on this page is provided for educational purposes and is accurate to the best of our knowledge at the time of publication. Status designations change as new assessments are conducted. We recommend consulting the official IUCN Red List website and the SASSi consumer guide directly for the most current information on any species.
