Caretta caretta — Loggerhead Turtle, KZN

Loggerhead Turtle

Caretta caretta ⚠ Vulnerable

The loggerhead turtle is KwaZulu-Natal's most iconic nesting sea turtle and a cornerstone of the province's marine conservation legacy. Named for their exceptionally large, powerful heads — housing massive jaw muscles capable of crushing hard-shelled prey — loggerheads have been returning to the beaches of the iSimangaliso Wetland Park for thousands of years, guided by the Earth's magnetic field back to the very beach where they hatched.

KZN's loggerhead nesting beaches between Maphelane and Kosi Bay represent the most southerly loggerhead nesting sites in Africa and among the most significant in the entire Indian Ocean. Ezemvelo KZN Wildlife has monitored and protected these nesting turtles since 1963, making this one of the longest-running sea turtle conservation programmes on the continent.

Shell Length Up to 1.2 m
Max Weight ~200 kg
Lifespan 70+ years
Nesting Season Oct – Feb
Eggs per Nest ~120 eggs
Incubation 60 – 80 days

Physical Description

The loggerhead's most distinctive feature is its disproportionately large head — broader and more robust than any other sea turtle species. This powerful head houses the enlarged jaw muscles needed to crush the shells of molluscs, crabs, and other hard-bodied invertebrates that form the bulk of its diet.

The carapace (shell) is reddish-brown to orange-brown on the dorsal surface, with a heart-shaped profile and typically five pairs of costal scutes. The plastron (underside) is creamy yellow. Adult females nesting in KZN average around 90 cm in curved carapace length, though larger individuals exceeding 1.2 m are recorded.

Loggerheads have two pairs of prefrontal scales between the eyes and a relatively small head in juveniles that grows proportionally larger with age. Their flippers are adapted for powerful, efficient swimming across vast ocean distances.

Behaviour

Loggerheads are largely solitary animals that spend the majority of their lives at sea, coming ashore only to nest. Females return to nest on the same beaches where they were born — sometimes after migrations of thousands of kilometres — guided by the Earth's magnetic field in a remarkable feat of biological navigation that science is still working to fully understand.

Nesting females emerge from the sea at night, laboriously hauling themselves up the beach to excavate a nest chamber using their rear flippers. After depositing approximately 120 eggs, they carefully fill and disguise the nest before returning to the ocean. A single female may nest three to five times per season, at intervals of around two weeks, before departing for years before nesting again.

Hatchlings emerge after 60 to 80 days, typically at night, and make their way to the ocean guided by the brightest horizon — a behaviour disrupted by artificial lighting from coastal development, which can disorient hatchlings and lead them away from the sea.

Diet & Feeding

Loggerheads are primarily carnivorous, using their powerful, heavy jaws to crush and consume hard-shelled prey that most other marine animals cannot tackle. This specialised feeding ability gives them access to food resources unavailable to other sea turtle species, reducing competition and allowing them to thrive across a wide range of coastal habitats.

In KZN waters, loggerheads feed on the diverse invertebrate communities associated with coral reefs, rocky reefs, and sandy bottom habitats. Juveniles in their early oceanic phase feed on jellyfish and floating organisms at the surface, while subadults and adults transition to bottom-feeding on benthic invertebrates.

  • Crabs, lobsters and shrimp
  • Molluscs (clams, mussels, whelks)
  • Sea urchins and starfish
  • Jellyfish and salps
  • Sponges and corals (occasionally)

Nesting & Reproduction

KZN's loggerhead nesting season runs from October through February, peaking in December and January. Females nest every two to three years on average, producing multiple clutches per season. Each nest contains approximately 100 to 126 eggs, and the incubation period ranges from 60 to 80 days depending on sand temperature.

A critical feature of loggerhead reproduction is temperature-dependent sex determination — the temperature of the sand during incubation determines the sex of the hatchlings. Warmer nests produce more females; cooler nests more males. Climate change-driven increases in beach sand temperature are increasingly skewing sex ratios toward females, threatening long-term population viability.

Ezemvelo KZN Wildlife's turtle monitoring teams patrol nesting beaches nightly throughout the season, tagging females, recording nest locations, and in some areas relocating nests to safe hatcheries to protect them from coastal flooding and predation.

Habitat & Distribution in KZN

Loggerheads nest along approximately 200 km of KwaZulu-Natal's northern coastline, from Maphelane at the mouth of the uMfolozi River northward to Kosi Bay near the Mozambique border. The core nesting area lies within the iSimangaliso Wetland Park — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — where beach habitat is protected from coastal development and artificial lighting. Sodwana Bay, Cape Vidal, and the wilderness beach areas between them are the most important nesting sites.

Outside of the nesting season, KZN's loggerheads disperse widely across the Indian Ocean. Satellite tagging has tracked individuals travelling to feeding grounds as far as the East African coast, the Mozambique Channel, and the waters around Madagascar. Juveniles undertake a trans-oceanic migration into the Atlantic and back before returning to KZN waters as subadults — a journey that may take a decade or more.

Threats to loggerheads in KZN include entanglement in fishing nets and longlines, plastic ingestion (mistaking floating plastic bags for jellyfish), beach erosion and flooding of nests due to rising sea levels, artificial lighting that disorients both nesting females and hatchlings, and predation of eggs by monitor lizards, crabs, and other animals. Ezemvelo's long-running monitoring programme has demonstrated population recovery over recent decades, providing one of the conservation success stories of the KZN coast.

Fascinating Facts

Magnetic Navigation

Loggerheads can detect the Earth's magnetic field and use it as a GPS system to navigate across entire ocean basins. Females imprint on the unique magnetic signature of their birth beach as hatchlings and use this information decades later to return to the exact same beach to nest.

60 Years of Monitoring

Ezemvelo KZN Wildlife has monitored loggerhead nesting on the iSimangaliso coast since 1963 — making it one of Africa's oldest continuous wildlife monitoring programmes. Data collected over six decades has transformed our understanding of sea turtle population dynamics and the effectiveness of beach protection measures.

Temperature Sex Determination

The sex of loggerhead hatchlings is determined not by genetics but by the temperature of the sand in which the eggs incubate. Nests above 29°C produce mainly females; cooler nests produce more males. Warming beaches due to climate change are producing increasingly female-skewed populations.

Lost Years

After hatching, juvenile loggerheads disappear into the open ocean for years — a period called the "lost years." KZN hatchlings are carried by ocean currents into the Atlantic Ocean, where they drift for up to a decade before returning to African waters as subadults.

Crushing Jaws

The loggerhead's massive head and jaw muscles generate one of the most powerful bites of any turtle species — capable of crushing the shells of hard clams, conch, and even horseshoe crabs. This gives them access to prey species that are off-limits to most other marine predators.

Most Southerly Nesting in Africa

KZN's loggerhead nesting beaches are the most southerly on the African continent. The warm Agulhas Current keeps these beaches warm enough for successful incubation — a critical factor that makes the iSimangaliso coastline unique and irreplaceable for the species' survival in southern Africa.
