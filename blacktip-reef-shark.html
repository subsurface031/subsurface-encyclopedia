Blacktip Reef Shark

Carcharhinus melanopterus ⚠ Vulnerable

One of the most easily recognised and frequently encountered reef sharks in the Indo-Pacific, the blacktip reef shark is named for the striking black tips and edges on all of its fins. Slender, fast, and agile, these sharks are a common and iconic sight along KwaZulu-Natal's shallow coastal reefs, tidal flats, and lagoons.

Despite their relatively small size compared to other shark species, blacktip reef sharks are highly efficient hunters and play a vital role in maintaining the health and balance of reef ecosystems. Their presence in shallow waters — sometimes in just centimetres of water — makes them one of the most visible and regularly observed shark species along the KZN coastline.

Max Length 1.8 m
Max Weight 13.6 kg
Lifespan ~13 years
Depth Range 0 – 75 m
Maturity Age 5 – 9 years
Litter Size 2 – 5 pups

Physical Description

The blacktip reef shark is a slender, streamlined shark with a short, rounded snout and large eyes. Its most defining characteristic is the prominent black tips and margins on all fins — the first dorsal fin, pectoral fins, pelvic fins, second dorsal fin, and the lower lobe of the caudal fin all carry vivid black markings, often bordered by a white stripe.

Their dorsal colouration ranges from grey to brownish-grey, with a pale to white underside. A distinctive white band runs along each flank, separating the darker upper body from the paler belly. These markings are most vivid in juveniles and can fade slightly with age.

They are relatively small and lightweight compared to most other reef sharks, with a maximum length of around 1.8 metres — though most individuals encountered in KZN waters are considerably smaller, typically between 1.2 and 1.6 metres.

Behaviour

Blacktip reef sharks are active, fast-moving sharks that patrol shallow reef environments with rapid, precise movements. They are known for their characteristic leaping behaviour — spinning or breaching out of the water, particularly when pursuing schooling fish — a spectacle that makes them a favourite among snorkellers and divers.

They are generally timid and cautious around humans, typically retreating when approached. However, they can become bolder and more assertive in the presence of food, and nipping incidents have been recorded in shallow water where swimmers are wading through groups of feeding sharks.

Blacktip reef sharks are not strongly territorial but do show site fidelity — individuals often return to the same reef areas repeatedly, and home ranges of neighbouring sharks frequently overlap. They tend to be more active at dawn and dusk when prey is most abundant.

Diet & Feeding

Blacktip reef sharks are highly active predators that feed primarily on small reef fish. They use their speed and agility to herd and chase fish into shallow water or against reef structures, where escape is more difficult. Their slender jaws and small, serrated teeth are perfectly adapted for gripping and consuming small, fast-moving prey.

They are known to feed cooperatively in groups, working together to corral schools of fish before striking. This coordinated feeding behaviour is relatively unusual among sharks and contributes to their effectiveness as hunters on busy coral and rocky reef environments.

  • Small reef fish (wrasse, mullet, grouper)
  • Squid and octopus
  • Crustaceans (crabs, shrimp)
  • Sea snakes
  • Small rays

Reproduction

Blacktip reef sharks are viviparous, with pups nourished via a yolk-sac placenta inside the mother's uterus. After a gestation period of 8 to 11 months, females give birth to small litters of 2 to 5 pups in shallow, sheltered nursery areas such as lagoons, tidal flats, and mangrove margins.

Newborn pups measure approximately 33 to 52 cm at birth and remain in shallow nursery habitats for the first months of their lives, where they are relatively safe from larger predators. Females give birth every one to two years.

The use of shallow coastal nursery areas makes this species particularly vulnerable to habitat degradation, coastal development, and pollution — threats that are increasingly significant along KZN's rapidly developing coastline.

Habitat & Distribution in KZN

Blacktip reef sharks are found throughout the tropical and warm temperate Indo-Pacific, from the Red Sea and East African coast across to Hawaii and the eastern Pacific. In KwaZulu-Natal, they are most commonly encountered in the warmer waters of the northern coast, including the shallow reefs and tidal flats of the iSimangaliso Wetland Park — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — as well as around rocky reef environments further south.

They are strongly associated with shallow water environments, frequently found in water less than 30 metres deep and sometimes venturing into water just centimetres deep on tidal flats. They occasionally enter brackish estuaries but are predominantly a marine species.

The blacktip reef shark is listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List. Primary threats include overfishing — both targeted and as bycatch — the shark fin trade, habitat loss through coral and reef degradation, and the destruction of coastal nursery habitats. Their shallow-water lifestyle and coastal distribution make them particularly susceptible to human activity along the shoreline.

Fascinating Facts

Acrobatic Leaper

Blacktip reef sharks are well known for leaping and spinning out of the water — a behaviour most commonly seen when they are herding schooling fish near the surface. These aerial displays make them one of the most exciting sharks to observe in the wild.

Shallow Water Specialist

Blacktip reef sharks are one of the few shark species regularly found in extremely shallow water — sometimes in less than 30 cm of depth on tidal flats, with their dorsal fins visibly cutting through the surface.

Fin Identification

Researchers use the unique shape and markings of the black fin tips to identify individual blacktip reef sharks — a non-invasive technique that has helped scientists map their movements, social behaviour, and site fidelity over time.

Cooperative Hunters

Unlike most sharks, blacktip reef sharks have been observed hunting cooperatively in groups — coordinating their movements to herd schools of fish into tight baitballs before attacking. This social hunting strategy significantly increases their success rate.

Reef Ecosystem Role

As one of the most abundant reef sharks, blacktip reef sharks play a critical role in controlling populations of small reef fish and invertebrates. Their removal from reef systems has been linked to significant disruption of local food webs and reef health.

Electroreception

Like all sharks, blacktip reef sharks possess ampullae of Lorenzini — sensory organs that detect the faint electrical fields produced by prey animals. This sixth sense allows them to detect prey hidden in sand or behind reef structures with remarkable precision.
