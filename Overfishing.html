Overfishing & Illegal Harvesting

Reef fish depletion, abalone poaching & bycatch on KZN's coast ⚠ SERIOUS — Ongoing Decline

KwaZulu-Natal's inshore marine fish populations carry the accumulated legacy of more than a century of intensive commercial and recreational harvesting. The fish communities that existed on KZN's reefs and in its estuaries at the beginning of the twentieth century — before systematic commercial exploitation, before the growth of recreational shore and boat fishing, before the development of sophisticated tackle and sonar equipment — were fundamentally different from what KZN anglers and divers encounter today. Large, long-lived predator species like the dageraad, musselcracker, and various grouper and rockcod species have been reduced from their historical abundances across accessible reef areas to fractions of what they once were. This process — the gradual reduction of baseline expectations for what a "normal" reef fish community looks like, a phenomenon fisheries ecologists call "shifting baseline syndrome" — means that many of the people most passionate about KZN's ocean have no frame of reference for what was lost before their time on the water.

Overfishing in KZN is not a single, simple problem — it is a layered one. Recreational shore and boat anglers, operating largely within legal frameworks but sometimes exceeding bag limits or retaining undersized fish, have contributed to the depletion of inshore reef species over decades of cumulative pressure. Commercial linefishing and trawl operations have added further pressure on a broader scale. Poaching — particularly the large-scale, organised criminal harvesting of abalone that has effectively eliminated perlemoen from most of the southern KZN coast — operates entirely outside the regulatory framework and responds only to enforcement capacity and market demand. And bycatch from both commercial fishing and the KZN shark net programme kills thousands of non-target animals annually, including protected and endangered species. Addressing overfishing in KZN requires different interventions at each of these levels simultaneously, and meaningful progress requires engagement and compliance from the fishing community itself.

KZN's Depleted Reef Fish Species

The list of reef fish species that have undergone significant population declines on KZN's accessible inshore reefs reads like a roll-call of the province's most iconic and prized angling targets. The red steenbras (dageraad), musselcracker (white musselcracker and black musselcracker), poenskop, various grouper species (rockcod, brindle bass), Natal stumpnose, and scotsman have all been substantially reduced from historical abundances by decades of fishing pressure. These are species characterised by late maturity — many do not reach sexual maturity until they are 5–10 years old — slow growth, and long lifespans, properties that make them highly vulnerable to overfishing because reproductive capacity cannot keep pace with harvest rates once exploitation exceeds a threshold level.

The pattern of depletion follows a consistent trajectory known as "fishing down the food web": as populations of large, high-value predator species are reduced, fishing effort shifts to smaller, less preferred species at lower trophic levels, which are in turn reduced, driving effort toward progressively smaller and less ecologically significant targets. On KZN's heavily fished inshore reefs, the fish communities that divers and anglers encounter today are measurably skewed toward smaller, faster-maturing species — a community structure that reflects decades of selective removal of the largest and most reproductive individuals. Size limits that are intended to allow fish to reach maturity before capture are frequently inadequate given what is now understood about the reproductive biology of KZN's key reef fish species, and are rarely enforced with sufficient consistency to achieve their conservation intent.

  • Red steenbras (dageraad): SASSi Red — do not consume; critically depleted inshore
  • Musselcracker (both species): SASSi Red — slow maturation, easily overfished
  • Brindle bass (grouper): SASSi Red — late maturity, low reproductive rate
  • Scotsman: SASSi Orange to Red depending on method — heavily targeted
  • Natal stumpnose: restricted to shallow estuarine and nearshore habitat — vulnerable
  • Pattern of depletion: large predators first, then progressively smaller species

Abalone Poaching — An Organised Crime Crisis

The poaching of South African abalone (perlemoen, Haliotis midae) is one of the most serious fisheries crime problems in South Africa, and the KZN coast has not been spared its consequences. Abalone is a large, slow-growing marine gastropod that occurs on rocky reef systems and is highly valued as a luxury food product in East Asian markets — particularly in Hong Kong and mainland China — where it commands prices of several hundred rand per kilogram. This market value has driven the development of a sophisticated, nationally operating abalone poaching industry that involves well-resourced criminal networks, violence against marine enforcement officers, and the capture of significant quantities of the drug nyaupe which is used to recruit and pay poor community members to dive for abalone.

The commercial abalone fishery in South Africa has been reduced or closed in multiple management zones due to severe stock depletion, but illegal harvesting continues to dramatically exceed legal quotas across the country. In KZN, abalone populations on the southern coast — already at the warmer, northern edge of the species' range — have been stripped from accessible reef areas to the point where most recreational divers encounter no abalone at all in areas where large populations existed within living memory. Recovery of abalone populations in the absence of ongoing poaching pressure requires many years given the species' slow growth rate; in the presence of continued illegal harvesting, recovery is functionally impossible. Reporting suspicious shore activity — divers collecting from rocky reefs without permits, vehicles loading into the water at night, unusual equipment on the rocks — to Ezemvelo KZN Wildlife's environmental crime hotline is the most direct public contribution to disrupting illegal abalone harvesting operations.

  • Abalone (perlemoen) commands high prices in Asian luxury markets
  • Organised criminal networks operate nationally — KZN included
  • Abalone stripped from accessible KZN southern coast reefs
  • Recovery requires many years free of illegal harvest — currently not occurring
  • Report suspicious diving activity: Ezemvelo hotline 0800 203 886
  • Nyaupe used to recruit community members to dive illegally — a social harm as well as ecological

Bycatch — The Unintended Catch

Bycatch is the term for non-target animals caught and killed in fishing operations — species that were not the intended target of the fishery, were not sought, and may not legally be retained, but die as a result of contact with fishing gear regardless. In KZN's marine environment, bycatch mortality occurs across multiple fishing sectors. The KZN shark net programme — a set of monofilament mesh nets deployed at 37 beaches between the Thukela River and the Natal South Coast — reduces shark attack incidents on protected beaches but in doing so kills a significant number of non-target animals including loggerhead turtles, Indo-Pacific bottlenose dolphins, humpback dolphins, manta rays, and juvenile sharks of species that play important ecological roles on KZN's reefs.

Commercial trawl fisheries operating in the waters off KZN's coast generate additional bycatch including juvenile fish of commercial species at below-legal sizes, seabirds that dive for fish around trawl operations and become entangled in net cables, and non-target invertebrates including coral fragments removed by bottom trawling in deeper reef zones. Line fisheries — both commercial and recreational — contribute through the accidental capture of protected species including turtles on longlines and stingrays on bottom-fishing gear. The cumulative bycatch mortality across these sectors represents a substantial and largely unquantified addition to the direct harvest pressure on KZN's marine wildlife. Consumer choices and political advocacy that supports the transition to more selective fishing methods — including the replacement of shark nets with non-lethal shark deterrent technologies such as drumlines, shark barriers, and personal deterrents — are the most accessible levers for public engagement with the bycatch problem.

  • KZN shark nets (37 beaches): non-selective entanglement of turtles, dolphins, rays
  • Commercial trawl: juvenile fish, seabirds, non-target invertebrates
  • Longlines: turtle bycatch a documented problem in KZN waters
  • Non-lethal alternatives to shark nets include drumlines and electric barriers
  • SASSi provides method-based ratings — line-caught vs trawl-caught matters
  • Consumer pressure for selective fishing methods supports industry transition

What You Can Do — Responsible Fishing & Seafood Choices

The SASSi (Southern African Sustainable Seafood Initiative) system is the essential tool for any person who eats seafood, fishes recreationally, or makes purchasing decisions about fish products in South Africa. The free SASSi smartphone app and website provide real-time ratings for fish species in three categories: Green (best choice — sustainably sourced), Orange (think twice — some sustainability concerns), and Red (do not buy or consume — overfished, illegally harvested, or caught by methods with unacceptable bycatch). The KZN angling community has significant power to accelerate the recovery of depleted species simply by choosing not to harvest Red-listed species, even where legal to do so, and by engaging with suppliers and restaurants to ask where their fish comes from and how it was caught.

Recreational anglers can make a direct and meaningful contribution to reef fish recovery by practising catch-and-release for depleted species, by adhering scrupulously to bag and size limits even when enforcement is absent, by reporting violations to the 1Marine environmental crime hotline, and by engaging constructively with the ongoing process of reviewing and updating KZN's recreational fishing regulations in line with the best available science on stock status. The fishing community is both one of the groups most exposed to the consequences of depletion and one of the groups most capable of changing the trajectory through coordinated behaviour change. Organisations including the KZN Ski-Boat Association and various shore angling bodies have programmes to promote responsible angling — participation in and support for these initiatives strengthens the community's own conservation capacity.

  • Download the free SASSi app — check species ratings before buying or ordering fish
  • Green list only: choose sustainable species at restaurants, fishmongers, and supermarkets
  • Recreational anglers: adhere to all bag and size limits regardless of enforcement presence
  • Practice catch-and-release for Red-listed species — even legal catch depletes stocks
  • Report illegal fishing: Ezemvelo KZN Wildlife hotline 0800 203 886
  • Ask your restaurant or fishmonger: how was this caught, and where?
  • Engage with angling organisations on responsible fishing standards

Overfishing in KZN — History, Shifting Baselines & the Path to Recovery

Understanding the full scale of overfishing on KZN's coast requires understanding the concept of "shifting baseline syndrome" — a term coined by fisheries scientist Daniel Pauly to describe the way each generation of scientists, fishers, and ocean users sets their baseline expectation of what a normal, healthy ocean looks like based on the conditions they first encountered, without adequate knowledge of what existed before their time. An angler who began fishing KZN's reefs in the 1990s may consider the fish populations they found then to be the natural baseline — unaware that those populations were already substantially depleted from what existed in the 1960s, which were in turn depleted from the 1930s. Each generation inherits a progressively degraded system and calls it normal. The result is that the accumulated losses become invisible — not because they are not real, but because there is no one left with direct memory of what was there before.

Historical records from KZN's early shore and boat fishing records — particularly the catch books maintained by early recreational fishing clubs, the records of the KwaZulu-Natal Sharks Board's fish bycatch data, and the oral histories of veteran KZN anglers — paint a picture of inshore reef fish abundance in the mid-twentieth century that is strikingly different from current conditions. Large musselcracker and dageraad were common catches from accessible KZN shore marks. Brindle bass of substantial size were regularly encountered on nearshore reef structures. Abalone was abundant enough to support a small-scale traditional subsistence harvest. These species are not gone — they persist in reduced numbers in the most remote and least accessible reef areas, in the inaccessible depths of offshore reefs, and within the most rigorously protected no-take zones — but they are no longer the normal, expected component of an accessible KZN inshore reef that they once were.

The path toward recovery exists and is supported by evidence from both KZN and comparable international examples. Marine Protected Areas with genuine no-take enforcement have been shown globally to increase fish abundance, average size, and species diversity within their boundaries within relatively short timeframes — 5 to 10 years — and to generate positive "spillover" effects on adjacent areas outside the MPA boundary as recovering fish populations expand into surrounding habitat. KZN's existing MPAs — the iSimangaliso marine protected area on the northern coast, Aliwal Shoal MPA, and others — provide the foundation for this recovery process, but their effectiveness depends critically on adequate resourcing of compliance monitoring and enforcement. Expanded public awareness of the link between fishing behaviour, seafood consumer choices, and the long-term trajectory of KZN's reef ecosystems is an essential component of building the public support that makes adequate conservation resourcing politically sustainable.

Shifting Baselines — The Invisible Loss

Each generation of KZN ocean users calibrates their sense of "normal" reef fish abundance from the conditions they first encountered. The cumulative depletion of reef fish populations over a century of intensive harvesting has happened incrementally — no single generation witnessed a catastrophic collapse — making the total scale of loss effectively invisible to anyone who did not have access to the historical record. The fish communities on KZN's accessible inshore reefs today are substantially less abundant, less diverse in large predatory species, and structurally different from what existed a hundred years ago. Recognising this is the first step toward understanding why current regulations and quotas, calibrated to current depleted baselines, may be insufficient to drive meaningful recovery.

The SASSi Traffic Light System

The Southern African Sustainable Seafood Initiative rates fish species sold in South Africa on a three-colour system: Green (best choice — sustainable method and stock), Orange (think twice — some concerns about stock or method), and Red (do not buy — overfished, poached, or unsustainable bycatch). The free SASSi app allows real-time consultation by species name, making it immediately usable at a restaurant, fishmonger, or supermarket. Species rated Red include most abalone, red steenbras, brindle bass, and certain trawl-caught species. The ratings are updated as new stock assessments become available. Downloading and using the app is one of the simplest and most direct individual contributions to reducing demand for overexploited KZN fish species.

No-Take Zones Work

The effectiveness of fully protected no-take marine reserves — areas where all forms of extraction are prohibited and enforcement is consistent — in recovering fish populations is one of the best-supported findings in marine conservation science. Reviews of global MPA performance consistently show that no-take reserves generate significant increases in fish abundance, average fish size, and species diversity within their boundaries compared to adjacent fished areas, typically within 5–10 years of establishment. The spillover of recovering fish populations into adjacent fished areas provides a direct benefit to the fishing community outside the reserve. KZN's existing MPAs have the potential to achieve these outcomes if adequately resourced — the limiting factor is enforcement capacity, not ecological potential.

Abalone Is a Red-List Species

South African abalone (perlemoen, Haliotis midae) is rated Red by SASSi — do not buy, consume, or order. Legal commercial quotas are small, tightly controlled, and frequently exceeded by illegal harvesting; the presence of abalone on a restaurant menu or in a seafood product in South Africa cannot be reliably attributed to a legal, sustainable source given the scale of the illegal trade. Consuming abalone in South Africa financially supports a criminal industry that is ecologically devastating KZN's reef systems and causing significant social harm in coastal communities through the recruitment of vulnerable people into illegal diving operations linked to drug networks. The single most effective consumer action on abalone is simply not to eat it.

Bag Limits Only Work When Followed

South Africa's recreational fishing regulations specify maximum bag limits and minimum size limits for key species — rules designed to ensure that a sufficient proportion of the population survives to reproduce before being harvested. These regulations are only effective as a conservation tool if they are consistently followed, including in the absence of an enforcement officer. Evidence from recreational fishing catch surveys in KZN consistently records non-compliance with bag and size limits at rates that significantly undermine the conservation intent of the regulations. Voluntary compliance by the majority of anglers is the only practical mechanism for achieving the intended protection in a coastline too extensive to monitor comprehensively. The fishing community is the primary enforcer of its own regulations through social norms and peer behaviour.

Reporting Illegal Fishing Matters

Ezemvelo KZN Wildlife's environmental crime hotline — 0800 203 886, toll-free and available 24 hours — accepts reports of illegal fishing, abalone poaching, and other fisheries crimes. Reporting is confidential and the information provided contributes to operational intelligence that shapes enforcement deployments. In areas where organised abalone poaching is active, community reporting of suspicious vehicle movements near reef access points, unusual nocturnal diving activity, and individuals offering abalone for sale at below-market prices provides critical information that enforcement agencies cannot gather without community cooperation. A single call reporting a poaching operation can prevent the illegal harvest of hundreds of animals from an already depleted reef.
