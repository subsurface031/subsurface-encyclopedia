Sphyrna lewini — Scalloped Hammerhead Shark, KZN

Hammerhead Shark

Sphyrna lewini (Scalloped Hammerhead) ⚠ Critically Endangered

Among the most visually striking of all shark species, the hammerhead shark is instantly recognisable by its uniquely flattened, laterally extended head — known as a cephalofoil. The scalloped hammerhead is the species most commonly encountered in KwaZulu-Natal's waters, and is one of the few sharks known to form large social schools, sometimes numbering in the hundreds.

Once a relatively common sight along the KZN coastline, hammerhead populations have declined dramatically due to targeted fishing for their highly prized fins, bycatch in longline fisheries, and habitat degradation. They are now classified as Critically Endangered on the IUCN Red List — one of the most threatened shark species in the world.

Max Length 4.3 m
Max Weight 152 kg
Lifespan ~30 years
Depth Range 0 – 512 m
Maturity Age 7 – 9 years
Litter Size 15 – 31 pups

Physical Description

The scalloped hammerhead's most defining feature is its distinctive cephalofoil — a broad, flattened head extension with a scalloped front edge, which distinguishes it from other hammerhead species. The eyes and nostrils are positioned at the outer edges of the cephalofoil, dramatically widening the shark's field of vision and sensory range.

Their body is slender and streamlined, grey-brown to olive-grey on the dorsal side with a white underside. The tips of the pectoral fins are often darkened. They have a tall, curved first dorsal fin and a strongly asymmetrical tail with a long upper lobe.

Compared to other hammerhead species, the scalloped hammerhead has a more pronounced central notch and lateral grooves along the front edge of the cephalofoil, giving it its "scalloped" appearance.

Behaviour

One of the most fascinating behavioural traits of scalloped hammerheads is their tendency to form large schools during the day, particularly around seamounts, islands, and oceanic reefs. These aggregations — sometimes numbering in the hundreds — are predominantly female, and are believed to serve social and reproductive functions.

At night, hammerheads disperse to hunt alone across open water and reef environments. They are highly active, fast-swimming sharks that undertake significant daily vertical migrations, diving deep during the night to hunt and returning to shallower warmer waters during the day.

Despite their size and imposing appearance, scalloped hammerheads are generally shy and wary of divers, and unprovoked attacks on humans are extremely rare.

Diet & Feeding

Hammerheads are versatile predators that use their wide, sweeping head like a metal detector, swinging it from side to side to detect the electrical fields of prey buried in sand or hiding in reef crevices. Their widely spaced sensory organs give them an exceptional ability to pinpoint prey with remarkable precision.

They are particularly adept at hunting stingrays, often pinning them to the seafloor with their cephalofoil before biting. Stingray barbs have frequently been found embedded in the mouths and heads of hammerheads — seemingly with no ill effect.

  • Stingrays and other rays
  • Bony fish (sardines, tuna, mackerel)
  • Squid and octopus
  • Crustaceans
  • Smaller sharks

Reproduction

Scalloped hammerheads are viviparous, meaning pups develop inside the mother and are nourished via a yolk-sac placenta — similar to placental mammals. This is a relatively advanced reproductive strategy among sharks.

Females give birth to large litters of between 15 and 31 pups after a gestation period of 9 to 10 months. Pups are born measuring approximately 42–55 cm in length and are immediately independent.

Nursery areas — typically shallow, sheltered coastal bays and estuaries — are critically important to juvenile survival. The loss and degradation of these habitats is a significant threat to hammerhead populations along the KZN coastline.

Habitat & Distribution in KZN

Scalloped hammerheads are found in warm temperate and tropical coastal waters throughout the world's oceans. In KwaZulu-Natal, they are most commonly encountered in offshore and deeper reef environments, including Protea Banks near Shelly Beach, where they are periodically sighted by divers. They tend to prefer water temperatures above 22°C and are most active in KZN's warmer northern waters.

Juvenile hammerheads use the sheltered bays and estuaries of northern KZN — including the iSimangaliso Wetland Park — as nursery grounds, where they spend their early years before venturing into deeper offshore waters as they mature.

Their populations have declined severely throughout their global range. In South African waters, the scalloped hammerhead is listed as a protected species, and fishing for or possession of hammerhead sharks is prohibited. Despite this, illegal finning remains a significant threat, driven by demand for shark fin soup in Asian markets.

Fascinating Facts

360° Vision

The wide placement of the eyes on the cephalofoil gives hammerheads a near 360-degree field of vision — they can see above and below simultaneously, with only a small blind spot directly in front of their snout.

Living Metal Detector

The cephalofoil is packed with electroreceptors called ampullae of Lorenzini, giving hammerheads an extraordinary ability to detect the weak electrical fields produced by prey hidden under the sand.

Stingray Specialists

Hammerheads are one of the few predators that regularly hunt stingrays. They pin rays to the seafloor using their cephalofoil before biting — stingray barbs found in their jaws suggest near immunity to the venom.

Social Schools

Unlike most solitary sharks, scalloped hammerheads form large daytime schools of hundreds of individuals. Position within the school is determined by size — larger, more dominant females occupy the centre.

Critically Endangered

Scalloped hammerhead populations have declined by over 80% in some regions over the past 25 years, driven almost entirely by shark fin trade. They are among the most threatened shark species on Earth.

Deep Divers

Despite being a coastal species, hammerheads have been recorded diving to depths of over 500 metres, likely following prey or seeking cooler, oxygen-rich water on their nightly hunting forays.
