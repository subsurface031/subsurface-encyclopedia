Isurus oxyrinchus — Shortfin Mako Shark, KZN

Mako Shark

Isurus oxyrinchus (Shortfin Mako) ⚠ Endangered

The shortfin mako is the fastest shark on Earth — a supreme evolutionary achievement of speed, power, and hydrodynamic efficiency. Built like a torpedo, with a conical snout, crescent-shaped tail, and a body adapted for sustained high-speed pursuit, the mako is the ocean's answer to the cheetah. Capable of bursts exceeding 70 km/h and able to leap up to 6 metres clear of the water when hooked or excited, this species commands awe from everyone fortunate enough to encounter one.

In KwaZulu-Natal, makos are primarily an offshore species, patrolling the deeper blue waters of the Indian Ocean beyond the continental shelf. They are periodically encountered by long-range game fishermen and occasionally by divers at offshore seamounts and pinnacles. Their global populations have declined severely due to the longline fishing industry, and the shortfin mako was uplisted to Endangered on the IUCN Red List in 2019.

Max Length 3.8 m
Max Weight 570 kg
Lifespan ~32 years
Top Speed ~74 km/h
Maturity Age 18 years (F)
Litter Size 4 – 25 pups

Physical Description

The shortfin mako is one of the most streamlined and hydrodynamically refined animals on Earth. Its body is spindle-shaped with a pointed conical snout, large black eyes with no visible pupils, and a mouth lined with long, smooth, blade-like teeth that are visible even when the mouth is closed — similar to the ragged-tooth shark but more slender and curved.

The colouration is a vivid, iridescent cobalt blue or purple-blue on the dorsal surface — one of the most striking colour schemes of any shark — with a stark white underside. This brilliant blue fades rapidly after death, turning grey-brown within hours, meaning photographs rarely capture the true vibrancy of a living mako.

The tail is crescent-shaped and nearly symmetrical — a hallmark of fast, pelagic swimmers such as tuna and billfish. Strong keels on either side of the caudal peduncle provide stability at high speed. The pectoral fins are short relative to body length, reducing drag and contributing to the shark's exceptional manoeuvrability.

Behaviour

Mako sharks are among the most active, energetic, and behaviourally complex of all shark species. They are endothermic — warm-blooded — able to maintain their body temperature several degrees above the surrounding water through a specialised countercurrent heat exchange system called the rete mirabile. This allows their muscles, eyes, and brain to function optimally in cold, deep water, giving them a significant advantage over ectothermic prey.

They are highly migratory, capable of covering vast distances across open ocean. Tagging studies have recorded individual makos travelling more than 5,000 km in a single year. In the Indian Ocean, they follow temperature gradients and prey availability, moving seasonally through KZN's offshore waters.

When hooked or threatened, makos are famous for spectacular aerial leaps — sometimes clearing the water by 6 metres or more — and sustained, powerful runs that make them among the most prized and challenging game fish in the world. They are considered potentially dangerous to humans, though encounters are rare given their offshore habitat.

Diet & Feeding

Makos are apex pelagic predators that rely entirely on speed and agility to pursue and capture fast-moving prey. Their primary prey in southern African waters includes bluefish, tuna, bonito, and swordfish — all fast, muscular species that few other predators can match in open water pursuit. The mako is one of the only animals capable of consistently catching and killing adult swordfish.

They attack prey from below and behind at high speed, delivering slashing bites with their long, curved teeth. Swordfish bills have been found embedded in mako shark bodies, testifying to the intensity of encounters with this large, aggressive prey species. Their smooth, unserrated teeth are adapted for gripping rather than cutting — prey is typically swallowed whole or in large pieces.

  • Tuna, bonito, and bluefish
  • Swordfish and billfish
  • Mackerel and sardines
  • Squid
  • Other sharks and dolphins (occasionally)

Reproduction

Mako sharks reproduce through aplacental viviparity — pups develop inside the uterus nourished by unfertilised eggs provided by the mother (oophagy), rather than through a placental connection. Litters of 4 to 25 pups are born after a gestation period of 15 to 18 months, one of the longest of any shark species.

Female makos do not reach sexual maturity until approximately 18 years of age, and reproduce only once every three years. This extremely slow reproductive rate — among the slowest of any shark — means that populations are virtually incapable of recovering quickly from overfishing. A female mako may only produce two or three litters in her entire lifetime.

This reproductive biology, combined with their high value to commercial fisheries (fins, meat, liver oil) and their tendency to take longline baits, has driven a population decline of over 50% globally in the past three generations — resulting in the species' 2019 uplisting to Endangered on the IUCN Red List.

Habitat & Distribution in KZN

Shortfin makos are primarily oceanic, epipelagic sharks found in open water from the surface to depths of around 500 metres. In KwaZulu-Natal they are most commonly encountered in deep offshore waters beyond the continental shelf edge, particularly in the warmer blue-water currents of the Indian Ocean. They are seasonally more abundant in KZN's offshore waters during summer months when surface temperatures are warmest.

Makos are occasionally encountered closer to shore around deep offshore banks, pinnacles, and submarine canyons where upwellings concentrate prey. Game fishermen targeting billfish and tuna off KZN's offshore reefs periodically hook makos, and they are sometimes sighted bow-riding vessels in deep water — a behaviour that highlights their extraordinary speed and curiosity.

In 2019, CITES Appendix II listings came into force for all mako shark species, regulating international trade. South Africa, as a signatory, is required to ensure that any commercial export of mako products is proven sustainable and legal. Despite this, makos continue to be taken as bycatch in South Africa's longline tuna and swordfish fisheries operating in the Indian Ocean, and meaningful population recovery remains uncertain without stronger international fishing regulations.

Fascinating Facts

Fastest Shark

The shortfin mako is the fastest shark on Earth, capable of sustained speeds of around 35 km/h and short burst speeds recorded at up to 74 km/h. This makes it faster than most open-ocean fish and one of the fastest animals in the sea.

Warm Blooded

Makos are endothermic — they generate and retain body heat using a countercurrent heat exchange system called the rete mirabile. This keeps their muscles, brain, and eyes warmer than the surrounding water, giving them a decisive speed and sensory advantage over cold-blooded prey.

Swordfish Hunters

The mako is one of the only predators capable of regularly hunting and killing adult swordfish. Swordfish bills have been found lodged in the bodies of mako sharks — evidence of ferocious battles between two of the ocean's fastest and most powerful animals.

Spectacular Leaper

When hooked or agitated, makos are famous for launching themselves up to 6 metres clear of the water in spectacular, repeated leaps. This aerial acrobatics — combined with blistering speed — makes the mako one of the most electrifying game fish in the ocean.

Iridescent Blue

A living mako's dorsal surface glows with a vivid, metallic cobalt blue that is unmatched among sharks. This striking colouration is produced by light-scattering cells in the skin and fades almost immediately after death — making the living animal far more spectacular than any photograph suggests.

Critically Slow Breeder

Female makos reach maturity at around 18 years, have a gestation of 15–18 months, and reproduce only every 3 years. A female may produce fewer than three litters in her lifetime — making the mako one of the most reproductively vulnerable of all shark species to fishing pressure.
