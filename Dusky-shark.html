Carcharhinus obscurus — Dusky Shark, KZN

Dusky Shark

Carcharhinus obscurus ⚠ Endangered

The dusky shark is one of the largest and most wide-ranging of all the requiem sharks, and one of the most commonly encountered large shark species along the KwaZulu-Natal coastline. Recognised by its bronzy-grey colouration, moderately long pectoral fins, and low, rounded first dorsal fin, the dusky shark is a powerful and wide-roaming apex predator of both coastal and offshore environments.

Despite being a familiar presence in KZN waters for generations of fishermen, divers, and ocean users, dusky shark populations have declined dramatically across their global range due to their exceptionally slow reproductive rate combined with intense pressure from commercial and recreational fishing. They are now listed as Endangered on the IUCN Red List — a status that reflects just how vulnerable this once-abundant species has become.

Max Length 4.2 m
Max Weight 347 kg
Lifespan ~40 years
Depth Range 0 – 400 m
Maturity Age 20 years (F)
Litter Size 3 – 14 pups

Physical Description

The dusky shark is a large, slender-bodied requiem shark with a moderately long, rounded snout and large, rounded eyes. Its overall build is more streamlined than the stocky Zambezi or copper shark, reflecting its ability to range widely across both coastal and open ocean environments. A low, inconspicuous interdorsal ridge runs between the first and second dorsal fins.

Colouration is grey to bronzy-grey on the dorsal surface, often with a faint bronze or dusky sheen — the origin of its common name. The underside is white, with a subtle white band along the flanks. The tips of most fins are dusky or darkened, particularly in younger individuals, though these markings are far less vivid than those of the blacktip reef shark.

The first dorsal fin is moderately sized with a rounded apex and originates above or just behind the free rear tips of the pectoral fins. The pectoral fins are long and moderately wide. Their upper teeth are broadly triangular and serrated; lower teeth are narrower and more erect.

Behaviour

Dusky sharks are highly migratory, undertaking extensive seasonal movements along the KZN coastline and beyond. They follow water temperature gradients and prey availability — moving northward along the coast in winter months in response to the cold Agulhas countercurrent that drives the sardine run, and dispersing offshore in summer as waters warm.

They are not strongly territorial and tend to be encountered singly or in loose aggregations rather than tight schools. During the sardine run, however, dusky sharks gather in large numbers alongside copper sharks, common dolphins, and Cape gannets to exploit the enormous sardine shoals — creating some of the most spectacular marine feeding events on Earth.

Dusky sharks are generally not considered highly aggressive toward humans, though their large size and predatory nature warrants caution. They are curious and will investigate divers and boats, but sustained or repeated attacks are rare. They are, however, frequently caught on recreational fishing lines and in beach protection shark nets along KZN's coast.

Diet & Feeding

Dusky sharks are highly generalist apex predators with a broad and opportunistic diet that shifts with season, location, and prey availability. Their wide-ranging habitat use — from shallow coastal reefs to deep offshore waters — means they encounter and exploit an exceptionally diverse range of prey species across their lifetime.

Juveniles in nearshore nursery habitats feed predominantly on small bony fish and crustaceans, while adults target larger prey including pelagic fish, squid, and other elasmobranchs. During the sardine run they feed intensively on sardines, often in the company of other large predators in high-energy multi-species feeding events.

  • Sardines and pelagic schooling fish
  • Tuna, mackerel and bluefish
  • Squid and octopus
  • Rays, skates and smaller sharks
  • Crustaceans and cephalopods

Reproduction

The dusky shark has one of the slowest and most conservative reproductive strategies of any shark species. Females do not reach sexual maturity until approximately 20 years of age — among the latest of any requiem shark. After mating, females undergo a gestation period of 22 to 24 months — nearly two full years — before giving birth to litters of 3 to 14 pups in shallow coastal nursery areas.

Following birth, females undergo a resting period of one to two years before reproducing again, meaning the interval between litters can be three years or more. Combined with a maturity age of 20 years, this makes the dusky shark one of the most reproductively constrained large sharks on Earth — a population can lose members far faster than it can replace them.

KZN's shallow coastal bays and estuaries serve as critical nursery grounds for juvenile dusky sharks during their early years. The protection of these habitats — and a significant reduction in fishing pressure — are considered essential for any meaningful population recovery.

Habitat & Distribution in KZN

Dusky sharks are distributed throughout the world's warm temperate and tropical coastal and offshore waters, making them one of the most wide-ranging of all requiem sharks. In KwaZulu-Natal they are found along the entire coastline, from shallow inshore reefs and surf zones to deep offshore waters beyond the continental shelf. They are one of the dominant large shark species in KZN waters and are particularly abundant during the annual sardine run when they aggregate in large numbers along the southern KZN coast between May and July.

Juveniles use the shallow, sheltered bays and estuaries of KZN as nursery habitats, particularly around the iSimangaliso Wetland Park system and the bays between Durban and the southern KZN coast. Subadults gradually move into deeper coastal waters as they grow, with large adults ranging widely across the continental shelf and into pelagic environments.

The dusky shark is listed as Endangered on the IUCN Red List following documented population declines of over 50% in multiple ocean regions. In South Africa, they are one of the most commonly caught species in the KwaZulu-Natal Sharks Board's beach protection nets, and significant numbers are taken as bycatch in longline fisheries operating in the Indian Ocean. Their extraordinarily slow reproductive rate means that even modest levels of fishing mortality can push populations toward decline — making conservation measures and reduced bycatch critical to the species' long-term survival in KZN waters.

Fascinating Facts

Slowest Breeder

The dusky shark has one of the slowest reproductive rates of any shark. Females mature at around 20 years, carry pups for up to 24 months, and rest for 1–2 years between litters — meaning a single female may reproduce fewer than ten times in her entire 40-year life.

Sardine Run Regular

Dusky sharks are among the most abundant large predators during KZN's annual sardine run. Hundreds congregate off the south coast between May and July, feeding alongside copper sharks, dolphins, gannets, and Bryde's whales in one of nature's most spectacular predatory events.

Long Distance Migrant

Tagging studies have shown that dusky sharks undertake some of the longest migrations of any coastal shark species — individuals tagged off KZN have been recaptured thousands of kilometres away, with some making round trips exceeding 3,000 km in a single year.

40-Year Lifespan

With a confirmed maximum lifespan of around 40 years, dusky sharks are among the longer-lived requiem sharks. This longevity, combined with their late maturity, means that every adult lost to fishing represents an irreplaceable decades-long investment by the population.

Encounters at the Net

Dusky sharks are one of the most frequently caught species in KZN's beach protection shark nets, historically accounting for a large proportion of all sharks caught. The KwaZulu-Natal Sharks Board has been working to reduce dusky shark bycatch through the introduction of Shark Spotters and SMART drumlines.

Deep Divers

While typically found in coastal surface waters, dusky sharks are capable of diving to depths of at least 400 metres. This depth range allows them to follow prey into the deep scattering layer at night and return to shallower waters during the day — a diel vertical migration pattern shared with many pelagic species.
