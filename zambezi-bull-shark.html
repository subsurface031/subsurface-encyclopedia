Zambezi (Bull) Shark

Carcharhinus leucas ⚠ Vulnerable

Known locally as the Zambezi shark — a name reflecting its documented presence far up the Zambezi River and other major African waterways — this species is globally known as the bull shark, a name earned through its stocky, powerful build and notoriously aggressive disposition. It is one of only a handful of shark species capable of surviving indefinitely in freshwater, and in KwaZulu-Natal it is the species most closely associated with river mouths, estuaries, and the murky inshore surf zone.

Widely regarded as one of the most dangerous sharks in the world, the Zambezi is responsible for a significant proportion of serious shark incidents in KZN. Its preference for shallow, warm, often turbid water — the same zones favoured by swimmers, surfers, and bathers — combined with its aggressive nature and powerful bite, make it a species that demands both respect and awareness from all ocean users along the KZN coast.

Max Length 3.4 m
Max Weight 316 kg
Lifespan ~32 years
Depth Range 0 – 150 m
Maturity Age 15 – 20 years
Litter Size 1 – 13 pups

Physical Description

The Zambezi shark is instantly recognisable by its broad, flat snout, heavily built body, and small eyes relative to its head size. It is one of the stockiest of all requiem sharks — short, thick, and muscular, with a body profile that is almost blunt compared to the sleek forms of many other large sharks. This build reflects its powerful, forceful hunting style.

Colouration is grey to grey-brown on the dorsal surface, fading abruptly to a white or off-white underside. The fins often have dusky or dark tips, particularly in juveniles. The first dorsal fin is broad and triangular, set well forward on the body. Its teeth are broadly triangular with serrated edges in the upper jaw, and narrow and pointed in the lower — an arrangement suited to both gripping and tearing.

Females are significantly larger than males, commonly reaching 2.4 metres or more. The largest confirmed specimens have exceeded 3.4 metres and 300 kg, though exceptional individuals may reach greater sizes.

Behaviour

The Zambezi shark has a well-earned reputation as one of the most aggressive and unpredictable of all shark species. Unlike many sharks that make a single exploratory bite before retreating, bull sharks are known for sustained, repeated attacks — a trait that makes encounters particularly dangerous. They are highly reactive to blood and low-frequency vibrations in the water and have been known to attack in water less than a metre deep.

They are largely solitary hunters but will aggregate around abundant food sources such as fish schools, river mouths after floods, or fishing activity. Feeding can trigger a heightened, excitable state in which the sharks become more bold and less predictable in their behaviour.

Despite their reputation, Zambezi sharks are not mindless aggressors — like all sharks, they investigate potential food items cautiously. However, their preference for turbid, low-visibility water and their tolerance of human-frequented nearshore zones creates frequent and sometimes dangerous encounters.

Diet & Feeding

Zambezi sharks are among the most opportunistic and wide-ranging feeders of any large shark species. Their diet reflects their ability to exploit both marine and freshwater environments — they consume prey from both ecosystems with equal facility. Juveniles in estuarine nurseries feed primarily on small fish and crustaceans, while adults target larger prey including other sharks and rays.

They are known to use a "bump and bite" technique — striking prey with their snout before biting — which may serve to assess or disorient prey. Their powerful, broad jaws generate one of the strongest bite forces of any shark species relative to body size, allowing them to tackle large, robust prey.

  • Bony fish (mullet, catfish, snapper)
  • Rays and smaller sharks
  • Dolphins and turtles (occasionally)
  • Squid and crustaceans
  • Carrion and refuse near river mouths

Reproduction

Zambezi sharks are viviparous, with pups nourished through a yolk-sac placenta. After a gestation period of 10 to 11 months, females give birth to litters of 1 to 13 pups in shallow, estuarine nursery habitats. Newborn pups measure 56 to 81 cm at birth and are fully independent from birth.

The estuaries and river mouths of KwaZulu-Natal — particularly the iSimangaliso system and the Thukela and Breede river estuaries — serve as critical nursery areas where juvenile Zambezi sharks spend their first years feeding and growing in the relative safety of shallow, brackish water, away from larger marine predators.

Like many large coastal sharks, Zambezi sharks are late to mature — females reach sexual maturity at 15 to 20 years of age — making their populations slow to recover from overfishing and habitat degradation. The loss and degradation of estuarine nursery habitats is considered one of the primary threats to Zambezi shark populations in KZN.

Habitat & Distribution in KZN

The Zambezi shark is found throughout the warm coastal and estuarine waters of KwaZulu-Natal, from the Mozambique border in the north to the KZN–Eastern Cape boundary in the south. It is most abundant around river mouths, estuaries, and harbour entrances — particularly the Thukela (Tugela) River, the Mfolozi, the Durban harbour entrance, and the iSimangaliso estuarine system near St. Lucia. These murky, productive environments provide abundant prey and serve as nursery areas for juveniles.

One of the Zambezi shark's most remarkable ecological characteristics is its ability to tolerate and thrive in freshwater. Documented in the Zambezi River more than 1,000 km from the sea, and in the Brisbane, Amazon, Mississippi, and Ganges rivers in other parts of the world, this species can survive indefinitely in fresh water by physiologically regulating its body's salt balance — a feat impossible for almost all other shark species. In KZN, juveniles have been recorded in the freshwater reaches of the iSimangaliso lake system.

Zambezi sharks are listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List. In South Africa they are captured in significant numbers in the KwaZulu-Natal Sharks Board's beach protection nets and drumlines. They face threats from directed fishing for their fins, meat and liver oil, as well as habitat degradation of the estuarine nursery areas on which juvenile survival depends.

Fascinating Facts

Freshwater Survivor

The Zambezi shark is one of the very few shark species capable of surviving indefinitely in freshwater. They achieve this through osmoregulation — actively controlling the salt concentration in their blood and tissues — an ability unique among requiem sharks and rare across all elasmobranchs.

Highest Testosterone

Male Zambezi (bull) sharks have the highest testosterone levels of any animal ever measured — higher even than male elephants in musth. This is believed to contribute significantly to their notably aggressive behaviour compared to other shark species.

Named for a River

In South Africa the bull shark is commonly called the Zambezi shark after documented sightings hundreds of kilometres up the Zambezi River in southern Africa. In other parts of the world the same species is known as the bull shark, Ganges shark, or Nicaraguan shark — always named for local rivers it inhabits.

KZN's Most Dangerous

The Zambezi shark is responsible for more documented shark attacks along the KwaZulu-Natal coastline than any other species. Its combination of size, aggression, shallow-water preference, and tolerance for murky water makes it the species most likely to encounter — and bite — humans in KZN's coastal zone.

Strongest Bite

Studies have shown that Zambezi (bull) sharks produce one of the highest bite forces of any shark species relative to their body size — a consequence of their broad, powerful jaws and heavily muscled head. This bite force is sufficient to crush the shells of sea turtles and the bones of large fish.

Estuarine Nurseries

KZN's river mouths and estuaries — including the iSimangaliso and Thukela systems — serve as essential nursery habitats for juvenile Zambezi sharks. Protecting these coastal habitats is critical to the long-term survival of the species in South African waters.
